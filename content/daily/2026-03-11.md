### 1. FlexServe: A Fast and Secure LLM Serving System for Mobile Devices with Flexible Resource Isolation

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-10T00:31:25Z | Yinpeng Wu, Yitong Chen, Lixiang Wang, Jinyu Gu et al.*

> FlexServe frames secure on-device LLM serving as a resource-isolation problem and lowers TrustZone overhead by making memory and NPU protection dynamically switchable.

This paper targets mobile LLM serving under a compromised OS, where ARM TrustZone can protect model weights and user data but usually adds large inference overhead. Its central idea is flexible resource isolation: memory pages and the NPU can be switched between protected and unprotected modes, then used inside a TrustZone secure-world inference stack. Around that mechanism, the system adds LLM-aware memory management, a secure inference pipeline, and a multi-model scheduler to reduce TTFT and multi-model workflow latency. The abstract reports prototype results with 10.05x average TTFT speedup over a TrustZone strawman, 2.44x over an optimized strawman, and up to 24.30x end-to-end gains for multi-model agent workflows. The main missing details are the exact threat model, device and NPU setup, baseline strength, and whether steady-state decode, memory, energy, and security costs remain acceptable.

If the mechanism generalizes, it offers a practical path to lower-latency, privacy-preserving mobile LLM serving and agent-style execution under hardware-backed isolation. A key caveat is that the evaluation is only described against strawman TrustZone baselines, so the real improvement versus strong implementations is unclear. Another caveat is that the design depends on ARM TrustZone and switchable NPU protection, which may limit portability and deployment scope across devices.

### 2. The $qs$ Inequality: Quantifying the Double Penalty of Mixture-of-Experts at Inference

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-09T21:48:04Z | Vignesh Adhinarayanan, Nuwan Jayasena*

> This paper argues that MoE models pay a structural inference penalty from routing-induced weight-reuse fragmentation and HBM pressure on the KV cache, and packages that tradeoff into a simple `qs` criterion against quality-matched dense models.

The paper targets a systems question that training-centric MoE discussions often miss: whether sparse activation still helps once long-context decoding becomes dominated by memory behavior rather than nominal FLOPs. It claims a double penalty at inference, where expert routing fragments microbatches and reduces FFN weight reuse, while large resident expert pools consume HBM capacity that would otherwise support the KV cache. The proposed `qs` inequality combines sparsity and a quality-equivalent dense size factor to predict when an MoE model is structurally worse than a dense alternative for serving. The abstract reports evaluations on DeepSeek-V3, Qwen3-235B, Grok-1, and Switch-C, including a claimed 4.5x throughput advantage for a quality-matched dense baseline over DeepSeek-V3 at 128k context, plus cases where very large MoE models become deployment-infeasible on clusters where dense models remain viable. The main missing detail is that the abstract does not make the hardware setup, batching regime, baseline matching procedure, or implementation fairness clear, so the operational scope of the claim still needs a full read.

If the criterion holds under careful evaluation, it gives LLM serving teams a compact way to decide when MoE should be treated as a training-time optimization and when dense distillation may be the more deployment-efficient path. A key caveat is that the abstract does not specify hardware assumptions, kernel quality, or scheduling details, so baseline fairness is still unverified. Another caveat is that the `q` quality-matching factor could be difficult to estimate consistently, which may limit how portable the inequality is across model families and tasks.

### 3. PIM-SHERPA: Software Method for On-device LLM Inference by Resolving PIM Memory Attribute and Layout Inconsistencies

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-10T05:39:03Z | Sunjung Lee, Sanghoon Cha, Hyeonsu Kim, Seungwoo Seo et al.*

> The paper treats PIM-assisted on-device decoding as a weight placement and layout conversion problem between prefill and decode, and proposes two software-only paths to avoid duplicated model memory.

The paper targets on-device LLM inference, where compute-heavy prefill and bandwidth-heavy decode make PIM attractive mainly for the decode phase. It argues that the practical blocker is not only GEMM offload, but two phase-transition mismatches: prefill prefers cacheable weight placement while decode needs non-cacheable placement for reliable PIM triggering, and host execution and PIM expect different weight layouts. PIM-SHERPA addresses this in software with DRAM double buffering, which keeps one PIM-aware weight copy and stages small cacheable buffers, and online weight rearrangement, which performs swizzled copies immediately before GEMM. The abstract reports Llama 3.2 results on a PIM emulation system with about 47.8-49.7% memory-capacity savings while maintaining performance comparable to a theoretical maximum. The main missing details are hardware fidelity, end-to-end overhead breakdown, and how well the method generalizes beyond the reported setup.

If the method holds on real devices, it could make PIM-backed on-device LLM decoding more memory-feasible without requiring hardware changes or duplicated model storage. A key caveat is that the evaluation is reported on a PIM emulation system, so transfer to real hardware is not yet established. Another caveat is that the abstract compares performance to a theoretical maximum, which leaves baseline fairness and achievable end-to-end gains harder to judge from the abstract alone.

### 4. DeltaKV: Residual-Based KV Cache Compression via Long-Range Similarity

*arXiv preprint | 2026-02-08T15:14:36Z | Jitai Hao, Qiang Huang, Yaowei Wang, Min Zhang et al.*

> A KV-cache method that stores token states as residuals against retrieved long-range references and pairs it with a sparse-aware serving stack to turn memory compression into runtime gains.

Long-context LLM deployment is increasingly limited by the linear growth of KV-cache memory, and prior compression or eviction schemes often trade off accuracy, compression ratio, and serving efficiency. This paper proposes DeltaKV, which uses long-range similarity and shared latent structure to encode KV states as residuals relative to retrieved historical references instead of dropping tokens, and it pairs that idea with Sparse-vLLM, a runtime designed for sparse and irregular KV layouts. The abstract claims that KV memory can be reduced to 29% of the original while maintaining near-lossless accuracy on LongBench, SCBench, and AIME. It also reports up to 2x throughput improvement over vLLM in long-context scenarios when the method is integrated into the sparse-aware engine. The main missing details are the cost of reference retrieval, the hardware and workload conditions behind the speedups, and how much of the gain comes from the compression method versus the serving-system co-design.

If the claims hold under realistic serving conditions, the method could translate KV-cache savings into longer supported contexts or higher throughput under fixed memory budgets, which is directly useful for production long-context inference. A key caveat is that the reference-retrieval step may add enough latency or complexity to offset part of the claimed system benefit. Another caveat is that sparse and irregular KV layouts can be highly hardware-dependent, so the reported speedups may not generalize across serving setups without careful kernel support and memory management design.

### 5. Entropic-Time Inference: Self-Organizing Large Language Model Decoding Beyond Attention

*arXiv preprint | 2026-02-08T19:13:26Z | Andrew Kiruluta*

> This paper proposes treating decoding entropy, rather than token position alone, as the main control signal for LLM inference, tying scheduler decisions, paged-attention pruning, and sampling temperature into one serving policy.

The paper argues that current LLM inference stacks optimize latency and throughput under largely fixed token-time decoding rules, while leaving uncertainty only indirectly used. It proposes an "entropic-time" inference scheme where online entropy becomes the shared control variable for scheduling, attention sparsification, and temperature adjustment, with a concrete integration path built on vLLM and paged attention. The claimed systems value is a more resource-aware decoder that spends compute where uncertainty reduction is highest and keeps generation near a target entropy regime. The abstract supports the existence of a design, pseudocode, and integration plan, but it does not make clear whether the system was implemented or quantitatively evaluated. The main missing detail is whether entropy can be estimated and acted on cheaply enough to deliver real latency, throughput, or quality gains over standard baselines.

If the control signal is practical, this could offer a unified way to manage latency-throughput-quality tradeoffs in LLM serving rather than tuning scheduling, sparsification, and sampling separately. A key caveat is that the abstract reads more like a systems proposal than a validated implementation, so the practical gains are unproven from the provided evidence. Another caveat is that online entropy estimation and target-entropy control may introduce overhead or instability that offsets any scheduling benefit, but the abstract does not clarify this point.

### 6. Efficient Fabrication of High-Aspect-Ratio Stepped-End Microneedles via Vision-Guided Assembly for Flexible Microelectrode Implantation.

*MICRO | 2025 | Xianghe Meng, Xingjian Shen, Yan Xu, Hui Xie 0003*

> This paper describes a vision-guided assembly pipeline for fabricating stepped-end microneedles from commercial wire and tube components to support flexible neural microelectrode implantation.

The paper targets a fabrication bottleneck for flexible neural microelectrode implantation: making high-aspect-ratio stepped-end microneedles without relying on expensive machining tools. Its main mechanism is a variable stiffness insertion workflow that combines a five-axis stage, gripping module, dual-camera feedback, and a finite state machine with low-stiffness and high-stiffness feeding loops to assemble tungsten wires and stainless steel tubes. The abstract reports three concrete outcomes: less than 32 um positional shift during microelectrode grasping, 2.3 times lower dura penetration force than flat-ended needles, and average manufacturing time under 4 minutes per needle. If these results hold under realistic process variation, the method could lower fabrication cost while improving throughput for implant tooling in neuroscience workflows. The biggest missing detail is how robust and reproducible the assembly is across samples, operators, and equipment tolerances, because the abstract does not make that evaluation setup clear.

If reproducible, the method offers a lower-cost and faster route to making implantation microneedles for neural interfaces without specialized beam or laser machining tools. A key caveat is that this is weakly aligned with the stated LLM systems and code-generation focus despite the generic domain label in the input. Another caveat is that the abstract does not clarify yield, statistical variation, or baseline fairness, so the practical robustness is still uncertain from the provided text alone.
