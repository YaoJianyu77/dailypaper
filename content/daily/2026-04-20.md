### 1. Faster LLM Inference via Sequential Monte Carlo

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-17T03:52:23Z | Yahya Emara, Mauricio Barba da Costa, Chi-Chih Chang, Cameron Freer et al.*

> This paper replaces speculative decoding's hard rejection with particle reweighting and resampling, aiming to turn rollback-heavy verification into a fixed-size parallel step for faster LLM inference.

Speculative decoding speeds up LLM inference by drafting cheap tokens and checking them against a stronger model, but its throughput drops when mismatches force early rejection and rollback. This paper proposes sequential Monte Carlo speculative decoding (SMC-SD), which keeps multiple draft particles and replaces token-level rejection with importance weighting and resampling. The systems claim is that LLM inference is memory-bandwidth-bound, so the extra arithmetic for parallel particle scoring can use otherwise idle compute while making verification more vectorized and fixed-size. The abstract reports a 2.36x speed-up over standard speculative decoding and a 5.2x speed-up over autoregressive decoding, while staying within 3% of target-model accuracy on reasoning, instruction-following, and coding benchmarks. The main missing detail is that the abstract does not make clear the hardware setup, particle count, baseline implementation quality, or where the approximation starts to hurt quality.

If the tradeoff holds in real serving stacks, this offers a concrete way to stabilize and extend speculative decoding gains when proposal-target mismatch would otherwise erase them. A key caveat is that the method is approximate, so quality loss may grow outside the benchmark mix summarized in the abstract. Another caveat is that the speedups may depend strongly on hardware characteristics, particle count, and proposal-target alignment quality with limited portability across deployments in the abstracted description provided here.

### 2. MemExplorer: Navigating the Heterogeneous Memory Design Space for Agentic Inference NPUs

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-17T12:29:54Z | Haoran Wu, Zeyu Cao, Yao Lai, Binglei Lou et al.*

> This paper presents a synthesizer that co-designs heterogeneous memory hierarchies and NPU parameters for agentic LLM inference, with separate attention to prefill and decode efficiency under power or performance constraints.

The paper targets a real systems problem: agentic LLM inference stresses both memory capacity and bandwidth, while prefill and decode phases pull the hardware in different directions. It proposes MemExplorer, a synthesizer that models multiple memory technologies across hierarchy levels and jointly selects memory-system choices and NPU design parameters such as matrix engine size for heterogeneous multi-device inference systems. The abstract claims sizeable modeled efficiency gains, including up to 2.3x higher energy efficiency than a baseline NPU and 3.23x higher than H100 for prefill-only workloads, plus up to 1.93x and 2.72x higher power efficiency for decode at matched performance targets. The practical value is in turning heterogeneous-memory design into a structured search problem rather than a fixed-architecture assumption for inference accelerators. The biggest missing detail is how realistic the workload model, baselines, and hardware cost assumptions are, and whether the gains survive under end-to-end serving constraints rather than phase-isolated comparisons.

If the methodology is sound, this could be useful for accelerator and serving architects who need a principled way to map LLM inference phases onto mixed memory technologies instead of relying on one-size-fits-all memory designs. A key caveat is that the abstract does not clarify whether results come from analytical modeling, cycle-level simulation, or hardware-backed evaluation. Another caveat is that comparisons against H100 may not be informative unless the workload setup, utilization assumptions, and system boundaries are tightly controlled and fair to general-purpose GPUs.

### 3. DepCap: Adaptive Block-Wise Parallel Decoding for Efficient Diffusion LM Inference

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-17T06:53:27Z | Xiang Xia, Wuyang Zhang, Jiazheng Liu, Cheng Yan et al.*

> A training-free block-wise diffusion LM decoder that adapts block size from cross-step influence and parallelizes only conflict-free tokens to improve the speed-quality trade-off.

Diffusion language models are attractive because they can refine a whole sequence in parallel, but practical inference still depends on how well block-wise decoding balances speed against quality. This paper argues that current methods use weak signals for two key decisions: where a block should end and which tokens can be decoded in parallel without harmful conflicts. DepCap is a training-free, plug-and-play method that uses the influence of the last decoded block to adapt the next block length and then selects a conflict-free subset of tokens for safe within-block parallel decoding; the abstract also says it is compatible with existing KV-cache strategies. The paper reports favorable speed-quality trade-offs across multiple DLM backbones and reasoning and coding benchmarks, including up to 5.63x speedup without significant performance degradation, and adds an information-theoretic argument for its block-partitioning rule. The main missing detail is whether these gains remain strong after accounting for control overhead, hardware setup, and baseline fairness in true end-to-end latency.

If the claim holds, this is a low-friction decoding-side path to better diffusion LM serving efficiency that can work with existing KV-cache optimizations instead of requiring model retraining. A key caveat is that the abstract does not say whether the speedups remain after including all scheduling and conflict-detection overhead in end-to-end latency. Another caveat is that hardware assumptions, benchmark protocol, and baseline fairness are underspecified in the abstract, so the reported trade-off needs verification from the full paper.

### 4. T-RBFT: A Scalable and Efficient Byzantine Consensus Based on Trusted Execution Environment for Consortium Blockchain

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-17T13:28:21Z | Wen Gao, Xinhong Hei, Yichuan Wang*

> A two-layer permissioned-blockchain consensus scheme uses TEE-assisted cross-group BFT and Raft-like intra-group agreement to reduce communication cost under mostly honest-node assumptions.

This paper targets consortium-blockchain consensus, where classical BFT protocols can be expensive when participation is restricted and faulty replicas are usually few. It proposes T-RBFT, which dynamically groups nodes by runtime characteristics, runs a TEE-assisted BFT protocol across groups, and uses an improved Raft-based mechanism inside each group. The abstract claims experiments showing lower communication overhead and latency plus higher throughput than existing two-layer consensus protocols. If those results hold, the design could improve scalability for permissioned deployments that accept TEE-based trust assumptions. The biggest missing detail is that the abstract does not explain the grouping policy, the TEE trust model, or the evaluation setup well enough to judge correctness, fairness, or generality.

If the assumptions hold, the method offers a route to more communication-efficient permissioned consensus by combining hierarchical protocol structure with hardware-assisted trust. A key caveat is that the dependence on TEE changes the trust model and may limit applicability where hardware trust is unacceptable or hard to validate. Another caveat is that the abstract underspecifies the improved Raft variant and dynamic grouping mechanism, so the real source of performance gains is not yet clear.* The evaluation claims are not detailed enough to assess fairness, reproducibility, or sensitivity to network and fault assumptions.

### 5. cuNNQS-SCI: A Fully GPU-Accelerated Framework for High-Performance Configuration Interaction Selection withNeural Network QQantum States

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-17T07:15:18Z | Daran Sun, Bowen Kan, Haoquan Long, Hairui Zhao et al.*

> This paper turns NNQS-SCI from a hybrid CPU-GPU pipeline into a fully GPU-resident distributed system by moving de-duplication, coupled-configuration generation, and memory management onto GPUs.

NNQS-SCI is accurate and scalable for neural network quantum states, but the existing hybrid CPU-GPU pipeline is limited by CPU-side global de-duplication and host-resident coupled-configuration generation. This paper presents cuNNQS-SCI, which moves the workflow onto GPUs through distributed load-balanced de-duplication, specialized CUDA kernels for exact coupled-configuration generation, and a memory-centric runtime with GPU pooling, streaming mini-batches, and overlapped offloading. The abstract claims up to 2.32x end-to-end speedup over a highly optimized baseline on 64 NVIDIA A100 GPUs while preserving chemical accuracy, plus over 90% parallel efficiency in strong scaling. The main systems value is the pattern of removing host coordination from an irregular multi-GPU pipeline and shifting the bottleneck back to on-device inference and memory handling. The biggest missing detail is how much each subsystem contributes, how fair the baseline is, and how dependent the results are on workload shape and A100-class hardware.

If the design generalizes, it provides a useful blueprint for moving irregular search-heavy scientific workloads off the CPU critical path and onto GPU compute, memory, and communication primitives. A key caveat is that the target workload is quantum SCI rather than LLM inference or serving, so transfer to token-generation systems may be limited. Another caveat is that the abstract does not show baseline fairness, subsystem breakdowns, or sensitivity to interconnect and memory configuration, so the source of the gain is still unclear from the abstract alone.
