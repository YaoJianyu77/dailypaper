### 1. Q-Hitter: A Better Token Oracle for Efficient LLM Inference via Sparse-Quantized KV Cache.

*MLSys | 2024 | Zhenyu Zhang 0015, Shiwei Liu 0003, Runjin Chen, Bhavya Kailkhura et al.*

> The paper argues that KV-cache sparsification and low-bit quantization interact in nontrivial ways, and proposes a token selection rule that accounts for both attention importance and quantization friendliness during LLM decoding.

This paper studies KV-cache compression for LLM inference, where long-context decoding is bottlenecked by KV-cache memory footprint and bandwidth traffic. The core claim is that combining cache sparsification with low-bit quantization is not straightforward: naive post-sparsification quantization can degrade heavy-hitter token selection, especially in deeper layers, and can preserve tokens that are poor candidates for quantization. To address this, the authors propose Q-Hitter, a token oracle that mixes accumulated attention scores with a layer-aware notion of quantization friendliness when deciding which tokens to keep. The abstract supports the qualitative claim that this should better preserve model behavior while reducing KV-cache cost, but it does not make the evaluation setup, hardware assumptions, or exact gains clear. The main reason to read further is to verify whether the method delivers real inference efficiency improvements under fair baselines rather than only a better token-selection heuristic.

If the method works as claimed, it could make KV-cache compression more practical by improving the quality-efficiency tradeoff for memory- and bandwidth-limited LLM serving. A key caveat is that the abstract is underspecified about evaluation details, so the practical serving gains are not yet clear. Another caveat is that the added token-selection criterion may introduce overhead that offsets some inference savings if implemented naively.

### 2. From Threads to Tiles: T2T, a Compiler for CUDA-to-NPU Translation via 2D Vectorization.

*CGO | 2026 | Shuaijiang Li, Jiacheng Zhao, Ying Liu 0055, Shuoming Zhang et al.*

> This paper presents a compiler that translates CUDA kernels to tile-oriented NPU programs by lifting SIMT thread parallelism into analyzable 2-D vectorized loop structure.

This paper targets the growing mismatch between CUDA s fine-grained thread model and NPUs that execute explicit 2-D tile instructions. It proposes T2T, a compiler framework that first rewrites implicit SIMT parallelism into a structured loop representation through a Unified Parallelism Abstraction, then applies 2-D vectorization to map selected loop pairs onto NPU tile operations. The abstract claims that this translation path also handles difficult CUDA features such as control flow and warp-level intrinsics through semantics-preserving optimizations. Reported evidence is fairly concrete at a high level: across benchmarks and representative NPU architectures, T2T reaches up to 73% of native CUDA performance on A100 and improves over translation baselines by up to 6.9x. The main missing detail is evaluation fairness and scope, including which kernels, which NPU assumptions, and how much of the gain comes from the vectorization strategy versus other lowering and optimization passes.

If the method generalizes, it could make existing CUDA kernels more portable to NPU-style accelerators and clarify how compiler structure, vectorization, and hardware mapping should co-design around tiled execution. A key caveat is that the reported comparison to native CUDA on A100 is informative but may not cleanly reflect end-target NPU efficiency. Another caveat is that the abstract does not specify the benchmark mix, baseline quality, or hardware realism of the representative NPU architectures used in evaluation, so reproducibility is unclear at this stage.

### 3. Numerical Superoptimization for Library Learning

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-25T20:47:15Z | Jonas Regehr, Mitch Briles, Zachary Tatlock, Pavel Panchekha*

> The paper reframes numerical library design as a search problem: mine candidate math primitives from superoptimization traces, rank them by counterfactual workload utility, and add the best ones back to the toolchain.

This paper asks a more upstream question than kernel superoptimization: not just how to optimize code given a fixed math library, but which new numerical primitives should exist at all. It presents GrowLibm, which repurposes the Herbie numerical superoptimizer to extract candidate primitives from search traces, deduplicate them through equivalence reasoning, and rank them by estimated utility on a target workload. The abstract reports gains across three scientific applications, including up to 2.2x kernel speedup at fixed accuracy, improved maximum achievable accuracy in at least one case, and a small end-to-end application gain when an LLVM matcher recognizes learned primitives in optimized IR. The practical value is a workflow for co-designing numerical libraries and compiler recognition rather than treating library APIs as fixed. The biggest missing details from the abstract are the search cost, workload dependence, and how robust the learned primitives are across hardware, compilers, and unseen programs.

If the method generalizes, it offers a systematic way to evolve numerical libraries and compiler pattern matching around real workloads instead of relying only on manual expert intuition. A key caveat is that the gains may be highly workload-dependent because candidate ranking is based on counterfactual utility over a given workload. Another caveat is that the approach depends on expert implementations of learned primitives, so the practical payoff may hinge on manual numerical-library engineering effort that the abstract does not quantify explicitly in detail or ease of generalization to other settings beyond the reported cases and workloads used here only rather than broader deployment contexts across diverse software stacks today widely enough for confidence or rigorous reproducibility assessment across toolchains and platforms under varying conditions over time significantly enough for broad adoption decisions in practice for teams evaluating maintenance cost versus benefit tradeoffs realistically at scale across ecosystems and production compiler environments with different optimization passes, numerical constraints, and integration burdens than those described abstractly here.

### 4. LLM-VeriOpt: Verification-Guided Reinforcement Learning for LLM-Based Compiler Optimization.

*CGO | 2026 | Xiangxin Fang, Jiaqin Kang, Rodrigo Rocha, Sam Ainsworth 0001 et al.*

> This paper trains a small LLM to propose LLVM IR peephole optimizations using reinforcement learning shaped by formal verification feedback, aiming to raise correctness enough for practical compiler use.

This paper targets a central obstacle in LLM-based compiler optimization: generated low-level rewrites are often invalid, which blocks practical deployment even when the model can sometimes find profitable transformations. The authors propose LLM-VeriOpt, which uses Alive2 semantic-equivalence checks as part of a GRPO reinforcement-learning reward to train Qwen-3B to generate LLVM IR peephole optimizations and learn from correctness feedback. The abstract reports materially better correctness than prompt-only use of the base model, strong rates of verifiably correct output, and speedups over O0 that are comparable to LLVM instcombine, with some cases surpassing it. If these results hold, the paper is a concrete example of compiler and verifier co-design rather than generic code generation. The main missing details are evaluation scope, baseline fairness, and how broadly the claimed gains generalize beyond the reported peephole setting.

If robust, this offers a practical path for using small LLMs in compiler optimization by coupling generation with formal correctness signals, which is directly relevant to safe low-level code transformation pipelines. A key caveat is that the abstract focuses on a narrow peephole-optimization setting, so the method may not transfer to broader compiler phases. Another caveat is that reported performance gains may depend heavily on benchmark choice and comparison methodology, which are not detailed here.

### 5. Oaken: Fast and Efficient LLM Serving with Online-Offline Hybrid KV Cache Quantization.

*ISCA | 2025 | Minsu Kim 0004, Seongmin Hong, Ryeowook Ko, Soongyu Choi et al.*

> This paper presents a hybrid online-offline KV cache quantization scheme for LLM serving, aiming to cut memory-bandwidth and capacity pressure without paying the usual online outlier-detection cost.

The paper targets a central LLM serving bottleneck: batched attention is constrained by memory bandwidth, while long-context workloads make KV cache capacity a growing systems problem. It argues that prior low-bit KV cache quantization methods preserve accuracy by handling outliers separately, but the online cost of detecting those outliers can erase the serving benefit. Oaken proposes a hybrid design that sets outlier thresholds offline and then uses them online for scale selection, paired with custom quantization/dequantization engines and memory-management support in the accelerator. The claimed value is a better accuracy-performance tradeoff for KV cache compression in serving systems, with possible reductions in bandwidth demand, memory pressure, and scaling cost. The biggest missing detail is evidence: the abstract is truncated and does not clearly state the evaluation setup, quantitative gains, hardware assumptions, or fairness against strong serving baselines.

If the method holds up, it could make long-context LLM serving more bandwidth- and capacity-efficient by reducing KV cache cost without adding prohibitive online processing overhead. A key caveat is that the abstract is truncated, so the actual evaluation evidence and scope are unclear. Another caveat is that the reported benefit may depend on specialized accelerator support rather than transferring cleanly to commodity GPU serving stacks.
