### 1. SpecAttn: Co-Designing Sparse Attention with Self-Speculative Decoding

*arXiv preprint | 2026-02-06T22:12:52Z | Yikang Yue, Yuqi Xue, Jian Huang*

> This paper proposes a self-speculative decoding scheme that reuses verification signals to choose a sparse KV subset for later draft steps, targeting long-context KV-cache bottlenecks.

The paper targets a central long-context inference problem: KV-cache growth makes decoding increasingly memory-bound. Its main idea is to co-design sparse attention with self-speculative decoding so that verification not only checks draft tokens but also identifies the KV entries that are critical for the next drafting step. That reuse of verification signals is intended to raise draft acceptance while avoiding a separate, expensive KV-selection pass. The abstract reports 2.81x higher throughput than vanilla autoregressive decoding and 1.29x over prior sparsity-based self-speculative decoding, which suggests the gain comes from better KV loading decisions with low selection overhead. The main missing details are the hardware setup, sequence-length regime, acceptance behavior across workloads, and how broadly the claimed lossless behavior holds.

If the method generalizes, it offers a practical path to higher long-context decoding throughput by cutting KV-cache traffic and KV-selection overhead through draft/verify co-design. A key caveat is that the abstract does not specify hardware assumptions, so the reported gains may depend heavily on a particular memory-bandwidth regime. Another caveat is that baseline fairness is unclear because the compared sparsity-based self-speculative methods and tuning details are not described in the abstract alone.

### 2. When RL Meets Adaptive Speculative Training: A Unified Training-Serving System

*arXiv preprint | 2026-02-06T18:28:54Z | Junxiong Wang, Fengxiang Bie, Jisen Li, Zhongzhu Zhou et al.*

> This paper turns speculative-decoder training into an online serving loop, using live inference traces and asynchronous RL to keep the speculator useful under real traffic.

Speculative decoding is already a practical LLM serving optimization, but current deployments usually train the speculator offline and only discover real system benefit after deployment. This paper argues that the separation creates slow time-to-serve, weak utility feedback, and poor adaptation when traffic or domains shift. Aurora addresses this by coupling an SGLang-based inference server with an asynchronous training server that learns from live traces, treating accepted tokens as positive feedback and rejected proposals as implicit negative feedback, while allowing hot-swapped speculator updates without interrupting service. The abstract claims 1.5x day-0 speedup on recently released frontier models and an additional 1.25x speedup over a static speculator under traffic shifts, which makes the systems angle directly relevant for serving. The biggest missing detail is whether these gains remain net-positive after accounting for trace collection, online training cost, swap overhead, and baseline fairness.

If the claims hold, this reduces the operational gap between model release and useful speculative serving while making the speedup target the real deployed system rather than an offline proxy. A key caveat is that the abstract does not show whether online learning overhead offsets part of the claimed serving gains. Another caveat is that using accepted and rejected tokens as RL feedback may be sample-efficient, but stability and convergence under non-stationary traffic are unclear from the abstract alone.

### 3. XShare: Collaborative in-Batch Expert Sharing for Faster MoE Inference

*arXiv preprint | 2026-02-06T23:33:07Z | Daniil Vankov, Nikita Ivkin, Kyle Ulrich, Xiang Song et al.*

> An inference-time MoE routing method that shares experts across a batch to cut redundant activations and reduce peak serving load without retraining.

MoE models are attractive because sparse expert activation can lower serving cost, but production batching and speculative decoding can activate too many experts and erode that advantage. This paper formulates batch-aware expert selection as an optimization problem and proposes XShare, an inference-time method that dynamically chooses shared experts for each batch by maximizing total gating score. The abstract claims up to 30% fewer expert activations under standard batching, up to 3x lower peak GPU load in expert-parallel deployments, and up to 14% higher throughput in speculative decoding through hierarchical, correlation-aware selection. If the coordination cost is small, this is a practical systems lever for improving MoE serving efficiency without changing model weights. The main missing details are output-quality impact, optimizer overhead, hardware and baseline setup, and how robust the gains are across heterogeneous workloads.

If the claimed gains hold with low overhead, the method offers a software-only path to better MoE throughput and lower peak resource pressure in existing inference stacks. A key caveat is that the reported gains may depend strongly on batch composition, request correlation, or deployment topology that the abstract does not specify. Another caveat is that forcing more shared experts across requests could degrade token quality or routing quality if it diverges too far from per-request routing decisions of the base model, though the abstract does not clarify this tradeoff explicitly, so this needs checking in the full paper rather than being assumed as an observed result from the abstract alone, which only states gating-score maximization and system gains without detailing quality measurements or evaluation criteria for output fidelity under collaborative expert sharing at inference time across heterogeneous request mixtures and speculative decoding settings, making this a central verification point for a careful reader of the full paper before trusting production use cases that are sensitive to answer quality or model behavior drift across domains or prompts or routing distributions in real workloads today and under future serving conditions as well overall for deployment decisions clearly and rigorously end to end thoroughly carefully and directly now and later in practice still definitely urgently critically importantly to validate claims properly fully comprehensively with actual evidence from the paper itself before drawing stronger conclusions about quality safety or broad applicability in production environments at scale across varying hardware and workload patterns.

### 4. KPerfIR: Towards a Open and Compiler-centric Ecosystem for GPU Kernel Performance Tooling on Modern AI Workloads.

*OSDI | 2025 | Yue Guan 0003, Yuanwei Fang, Keren Zhou 0001, Corbin Robeck et al.*

> A compiler-integrated GPU profiling framework that turns performance tooling into reusable compiler passes inside Triton for modern AI kernels.

This paper argues that modern AI GPU kernels need performance tooling that lives inside the compiler rather than beside it, because external profilers do not naturally preserve compiler structure or expose fine-grained optimization behavior. It presents KPerfIR, a multilevel compiler-centric profiling infrastructure where profiling functionality is implemented as compiler passes, and demonstrates the approach through integration with Triton. The claimed benefit is finer-grained analysis of issues such as overlap among function units inside a kernel, with the abstract reporting 8.2% overhead, 2% relative error, and actionable optimization insight. If that interface is usable, the main value is better co-design between AI compilers and performance analysis for generated GPU kernels. The biggest missing detail is scope: the abstract does not make the evaluated workloads, hardware coverage, baseline methodology, or portability beyond the Triton setting clear.

If the abstraction holds up, this could make GPU kernel observability more programmable and better aligned with compiler-driven optimization, which is relevant for generated kernels in LLM and other AI systems. A key caveat is that the abstract over-claims openness and portability without specifying how much of the design depends on Triton internals. Another caveat is that the reported accuracy and overhead numbers are hard to judge without workload, hardware, and baseline details, so fairness remains unclear from the abstract alone.

### 5. CuAsmRL: Optimizing GPU SASS Schedules via Deep Reinforcement Learning.

*CGO | 2025 | Guoliang He, Eiko Yoneki*

> This paper casts post-compilation GPU SASS scheduling as a reinforcement learning search problem that mutates compiler-produced schedules and reports additional throughput gains on specialized CUDA kernels.

Specialized CUDA kernels for LLM-scale workloads can still leave performance headroom at the final SASS scheduling stage, and exploiting that headroom usually depends on expert manual tuning. This paper formulates SASS rescheduling as an assembly game that starts from an -O3 schedule and lets an RL agent iteratively apply schedule mutations using throughput-based rewards from GPU execution. The abstract claims the method can be integrated into existing compiler frameworks and can improve existing specialized kernels by up to 26% and 9% on average. If that result is robust, the practical value is a post-codegen path to recover assembly-level speedups without repeated human trial-and-error. The main missing detail is evaluation scope: the abstract does not make clear which kernels, GPU architectures, search cost, or baseline comparisons support the reported gains.

If the method generalizes, it could give compiler or kernel-generation stacks a transparent post-pass that captures assembly-level performance gains without requiring human SASS expertise. A key caveat is that the abstract is underspecified on hardware, kernel mix, and evaluation protocol, so generality is unclear. Another caveat is that a throughput-reward search loop may be expensive or noisy relative to the speedups it finds at deployment time only once or infrequently, which the abstract does not clarify clearly to engineers assessing system tradeoffs overall today carefully here significantly and concretely now beyond the reported averages alone in this summary note for judgment purposes only, so the practical cost-benefit remains unclear until the full paper is read in detail with the full setup and methodology described properly and reproducibly end to end across hardware and workloads used by the authors in their experiments and analysis sections later on for readers evaluating deployment value and reproducibility limits in actual compiler pipelines and kernel-tuning workflows under realistic engineering constraints and budgets over time across teams and devices in production-like settings under repeated search runs and retuning needs.

### 6. Tackling the Matrix Multiplication Micro-Kernel Generation with Exo.

*CGO | 2024 | Adrián Castelló 0001, Julian Bellavita, Grace Dinh, Yuka Ikarashi et al.*

> A compiler-oriented method for generating GEMM micro-kernels in Exo that aims to approach hand-written intrinsics or assembly while reducing retargeting effort.

High-performance GEMM still depends on hardware-specific micro-kernels because the last layer of performance comes from carefully matched tiling, data movement, and instruction use. This paper argues that Exo can express a step-by-step workflow for generating those micro-kernels and reach performance close to, or sometimes above, manually developed intrinsic or assembly implementations. The practical mechanism is a concise library-based description of target instructions, which is meant to move retargeting effort from rewriting kernels to specifying hardware capabilities. If that holds, the work matters as a more systematic route to performance-portable low-level kernel generation. The abstract does not make the evaluation setup, target architectures, or baseline fairness clear, so the real portability and performance envelope is still underspecified.

If the approach generalizes, it could make low-level kernel retargeting more systematic and cheaper than maintaining separate handwritten micro-kernels for each hardware target. A key caveat is that the abstract does not show how broad the hardware coverage is, so the portability claim may be narrow in practice. Another caveat is that the Exo workflow may still depend on expert-crafted schedules or instruction descriptions that keep the manual effort high despite cleaner abstractions.
