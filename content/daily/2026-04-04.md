### 1. Goose: Anisotropic Speculation Trees for Training-Free Speculative Decoding

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-02T13:48:42Z | Tao Jin, Phuong Minh Nguyen, Naoya Inoue*

> This paper argues that training-free speculative decoding should use an asymmetric tree when draft-token sources have very different acceptance rates, and proposes an adaptive spine-plus-branches design to exploit that gap.

The paper studies training-free speculative decoding under a fixed verification budget, where tree depth and branch width trade off against each other. Its core claim is that common draft sources are not equally reliable: context-matched n-gram copies and statistical predictions from prior passes have sharply different acceptance rates, so balanced trees waste budget by treating them too similarly. Based on that observation, the method builds an anisotropic speculation tree with a deep spine of high-acceptance tokens and wide side branches of lower-confidence alternatives, aiming to increase accepted tokens per verification step without extra training. The abstract reports both a proof that this mixed tree is at least as good as using either source alone and empirical lossless speedups of 1.9-4.3x, with 12-33% gains over balanced-tree baselines under the same budget. The main missing detail from the abstract is how the adaptive policy is implemented in practice across workloads, including its overheads, robustness, and baseline fairness.

If the method is robust, it offers a deployment-friendly way to improve speculative decoding throughput without retraining models, mainly by spending verification budget where token acceptance is most likely. A key caveat is that the reported gains may depend strongly on workloads where context-matched tokens are unusually reliable, limiting generality. Another caveat is that the abstract does not clarify the runtime cost of adaptively constructing and managing the anisotropic tree, so net serving benefit needs verification in implementation details, hardware setup, and sequence-length regimes.

### 2. FlatAttention: Dataflow and Fabric Collectives Co-Optimization for Large Attention-Based Model Inference on Tile-Based Accelerators

*Submitted to IEEE Transactions on Computers. 14 pages | 2026-04-02T14:45:19Z | Chi Zhang, Luca Colagrande, Renzo Andri, Luca Benini*

> This paper proposes an attention dataflow for tile-based accelerators that reduces HBM traffic by pushing more communication into on-chip fabric collectives and reports substantial inference gains for modern attention workloads.

This paper targets a systems bottleneck that is becoming more important in MoE inference: attention now consumes a large share of runtime, while tile-based accelerators create new data-movement constraints across on-chip fabric and HBM. The core idea is FlatAttention, a dataflow that co-optimizes attention execution with fabric-level collective primitives so more communication stays on chip and expensive HBM accesses are reduced. The abstract reports high utilization, lower HBM traffic, speedups over FlashAttention-3 and GH200-based attention implementations, and end-to-end decoding gains for DeepSeek-v3 FP8 on a wafer-scale multi-die system. The practical value is clear for accelerator and runtime designers working on large-model inference, especially where memory traffic and inter-tile coordination dominate efficiency. The biggest missing detail is baseline fairness and implementation scope: the abstract does not make clear which attention variants, sequence regimes, mapping constraints, and hardware assumptions are most responsible for the reported gains.

If the claims hold under fair baselines, the work is useful for co-designing accelerator fabrics, runtimes, and attention kernels that convert on-chip communication support into real inference throughput and latency gains. A key caveat is that the reported gains may depend heavily on specialized tile-fabric collectives that are not available on mainstream accelerators. Another caveat is that the abstract does not clarify baseline fairness or how much engineering optimization the comparison implementations received, which could materially affect the headline speedups.

### 3. From Threads to Tiles: T2T, a Compiler for CUDA-to-NPU Translation via 2D Vectorization.

*CGO | 2026 | Shuaijiang Li, Jiacheng Zhao, Ying Liu 0055, Shuoming Zhang et al.*

> This paper presents a compiler that rewrites CUDA kernels into an explicit loop form and then maps selected loop pairs onto 2-D NPU tile instructions to bridge the SIMT-to-tile semantic gap.

This paper targets the growing mismatch between CUDA s fine-grained thread model and NPUs that execute coarse-grained 2-D tile operations. It proposes T2T, a compiler framework that first converts implicit SIMT parallelism into an analyzable loop-nest representation through a Unified Parallelism Abstraction, then applies 2-D vectorization to map chosen loop pairs onto NPU tile instructions. The abstract claims semantics-preserving handling of difficult CUDA features such as control flow and warp-level intrinsics, and reports both strong gains over baseline translation methods and partial recovery of native CUDA performance. If the method is robust, it could matter for retargeting existing CUDA kernels onto emerging NPU architectures without manual rewrites. The biggest missing detail is how broad the supported CUDA subset is and how fair and representative the evaluation setup is across kernels and target NPUs.

If the compilation strategy generalizes, it offers a plausible path to reuse CUDA kernel ecosystems on NPU-like accelerators while making the performance tradeoffs of thread-to-tile lowering more systematic. A key caveat is that the abstract does not clarify how much of CUDA is covered beyond the showcased optimizations, so portability claims may depend on a restricted kernel subset. Another caveat is that the reported comparison mixes native CUDA and translation baselines, which needs careful reading to judge fairness and practical significance on real NPU hardware targets or models of them .

### 4. GPU-RMQ: Accelerating Range Minimum Queries on Modern GPUs

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-02T09:23:08Z | Lara Kreis, Justus Henneberg, Valentin Henkys, Felix Schuhknecht et al.*

> This paper presents a hybrid GPU range-minimum-query design that combines hierarchical summaries with both ray-tracing and CUDA execution paths to reduce memory overhead and improve throughput on large arrays.

This paper studies range minimum queries on modern GPUs, where prior GPU and ray-tracing-based methods appear to run into a poor tradeoff between memory use, build cost, and query throughput at larger scales. The proposed system, GPU-RMQ, builds a hierarchy of range-minimum summaries in parallel and answers queries by scanning only the relevant hierarchy regions, while using ray-tracing cores and CUDA cores at different levels. The abstract claims this hybrid design improves the main systems bottlenecks by lowering memory footprint, greatly reducing index construction time, and increasing throughput, especially on larger arrays. Reported evidence includes comparisons against prior GPU approaches and an optimized CPU baseline, with substantial claimed gains in throughput and build speed. The main missing detail is how the hierarchy is laid out and scheduled in practice, including hardware assumptions, workload characteristics, and whether the comparisons remain fair across query distributions and memory budgets.

If the claims hold under realistic workloads, the paper offers a more scalable GPU RMQ design that could make large-array query processing more practical on current NVIDIA hardware. A key caveat is that this is not an LLM inference paper, so the relevance to the stated research focus is indirect and mainly at the GPU systems level. Another caveat is that the abstract reports strong speedups, but the exact workload mix, batching strategy, and fairness of baselines are not clear from the summary alone.

### 5. A Case For Host Code Guided GPU Data Race Detector

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-02T14:35:49Z | Ajay Nayak, Anubhab Ghosh, Arkaprava Basu*

> This paper argues that GPU race detection improves materially when static analysis uses host-side kernel launch semantics to constrain what kernel parameters can actually be at runtime.

This paper targets a familiar static-analysis problem in GPU software: dynamic race detectors miss bugs that do not manifest in tests, while static tools over-report because they reason over infeasible parameter values. The core idea is to analyze host launch code together with GPU kernels so the detector can infer which kernel arguments are actually possible during execution. The abstract claims this holistic CPU+GPU analysis, implemented as HGRD, finds a broad set of true races, misses none that prior dynamic tools miss, and eliminates the false alarms produced by prior static methods. If that holds, the value is better bug-finding precision for GPU kernel development and generated CUDA code, where false positives can make static tooling unusable. The biggest missing detail is evaluation scope: the abstract does not make clear the benchmark set, supported language features, analysis cost, or how robust the result is outside the tested programs.

If the claims generalize, this could make static race detection practical for CUDA-style development workflows by improving precision enough that developers can trust the warnings. A key caveat is that the abstract makes unusually strong correctness claims without enough detail on dataset size, ground truth construction, or threat model. Another caveat is that results may depend on restricted host-code patterns or kernel interfaces that are easier to analyze than real production stacks use today.

### 6. LLM-VeriOpt: Verification-Guided Reinforcement Learning for LLM-Based Compiler Optimization.

*CGO | 2026 | Xiangxin Fang, Jiaqin Kang, Rodrigo Rocha, Sam Ainsworth 0001 et al.*

> This paper trains a small LLM for LLVM IR peephole optimization using reinforcement learning with Alive2 verification feedback, aiming to improve optimization correctness rather than relying on prompting alone.

This paper targets a core bottleneck in LLM-based compiler optimization: generated low-level rewrites are often incorrect, which makes iterative improvement hard and limits practical use. The authors propose LLM-VeriOpt, a verification-guided reinforcement learning setup that uses Alive2 semantic-equivalence signals inside a GRPO reward to train Qwen-3B as an LLVM IR peephole optimizer. The abstract claims substantially better correctness than prompt-only use of the base model, strong verified output rates, and runtime gains comparable to LLVM's handwritten instcombine pass, with some cases exceeding it. For systems readers, the main interest is the compiler-and-verifier co-design loop rather than generic code generation. The biggest missing details are how broad the evaluation is, how fair the baselines are, and which optimization classes and hardware or benchmark settings drive the reported gains.

If the claims hold, this suggests a practical path to making small LLMs useful for verified low-level optimization by coupling model training tightly to compiler correctness tooling. A key caveat is that the abstract emphasizes correctness improvements, but the generality across workloads and rewrite families is not clear. Another caveat is that speedup claims may depend heavily on benchmark choice and comparison against O0, which is a weak runtime baseline by itself when judging optimizer quality fully through the abstract alone.
