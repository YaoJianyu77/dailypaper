### 1. ParisKV: Fast and Drift-Robust KV-Cache Retrieval for Long-Context LLMs

*25 pages, 16 figures. Under review | 2026-02-07T22:26:45Z | Yanlin Qi, Xinhang Chen, Huiqiang Jiang, Qitong Wang et al.*

> A GPU-native KV-cache retrieval system for long-context decoding that combines collision-based candidate selection with quantized reranking to stay robust under drift and scale to million-token contexts.

This paper targets a current bottleneck in long-context LLM inference: retrieving useful KV states fast enough to avoid the latency and memory costs of full attention. It proposes a two-stage retrieval stack, using collision-based candidate selection and then a quantized inner-product reranking estimator, with Unified Virtual Addressing support so CPU-offloaded KV caches can be fetched on demand at million-token scale. The abstract claims that this design preserves or improves quality relative to full attention on long-input and long-generation benchmarks while also improving decoding efficiency, including batch-1 speed parity or better, higher throughput in full attention's runnable range, and much lower latency than prior KV top-k retrieval baselines at million-token scale. If these claims hold, the main value is a practical path to long-context serving that reduces memory pressure without giving up decode quality. The main missing detail is evaluation clarity: the abstract does not explain the exact drift setting, benchmark mix, hardware assumptions, or how retrieval quality and system gains trade off across workloads.

The practical payoff is a retrieval-based decode path that could make long-context inference cheaper and more scalable while remaining competitive with full attention on output quality. A key caveat is that the abstract is strong on headline gains but weak on evaluation detail, so hardware assumptions and baseline fairness need verification. Another caveat is that quality claims may depend heavily on workload mix, top-k settings, or reranking details that are not exposed here.

### 2. LycheeCluster: Efficient Long-Context Inference with Structure-Aware Chunking and Hierarchical KV Indexing

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-09T14:50:35Z | Dongfang Li, Zixuan Liu, Gang Lin, Baotian Hu et al.*

> LycheeCluster proposes boundary-aware chunking and hierarchical KV indexing to replace linear KV-cache scans with logarithmic-time pruning for long-context LLM inference.

Long-context LLM inference is bottlenecked by attention cost and growing KV-cache memory, and retrieval-based KV management often trades away semantic coherence with fixed-size chunks while still scanning too much state. This paper proposes LycheeCluster, which uses boundary-aware chunking to preserve local semantics and a recursive hierarchical index based on triangle-inequality pruning to make cache retrieval logarithmic rather than linear, with lazy updates for streaming generation. The abstract claims up to 3.6x end-to-end inference speedup with negligible model-performance degradation and says it outperforms Quest and ClusterKV. If that result holds, the practical value is lower retrieval overhead for long-context serving without a large quality penalty. The biggest missing details are the hardware and workload setup, the cost of maintaining the index, and how fair the baseline comparisons are under real streaming conditions.

If the method generalizes, it offers a serving-side way to reduce long-context KV retrieval overhead and latency without changing the base model. A key caveat is that the abstract does not show how index build and maintenance costs scale with very long or rapidly changing contexts. Another caveat is that the claimed negligible degradation is underspecified because the tasks and quality metrics are not described in the abstract.

### 3. Deterministic Differentiable Structured Pruning for Large Language Models

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-09T07:59:17Z | Weiyu Huang, Pengle Zhang, Xiaolu Zhang, Jun Zhou et al.*

> A deterministic alternative to hard-concrete structured pruning that claims lower deployment mismatch and real vLLM serving gains on dense and MoE LLMs.

This paper targets structured pruning for LLM inference, where compression only matters if removed components translate into actual serving savings. It replaces the usual stochastic hard-concrete relaxation with a deterministic differentiable surrogate for the discrete l0 objective, with the stated goal of improving mask expressiveness, reducing train-test mismatch, and converging faster. The abstract says the method is applied to several dense and MoE models, including Qwen3-32B and Qwen3-30B-A3B, with downstream performance loss as small as 1% and stronger results than prior methods at 20% sparsity. It also claims end-to-end inference speedups in realistic vLLM deployments, which makes it more relevant to systems readers than pruning work that stops at proxy sparsity metrics. The main missing detail is that the abstract does not specify the pruned structures, hardware setup, or baseline protocol, so the source of the reported serving gains still needs verification.

If the deployment claims hold, the method could make structured pruning more credible for production LLM serving by turning sparsity optimization into measurable inference gains rather than paper-only compression improvements. A key caveat is that the abstract does not specify which structures are pruned, so it is hard to judge whether the speedups come from runtime-relevant changes. Another caveat is that hardware assumptions and serving configuration are omitted, which makes the reported vLLM gains difficult to interpret or reproduce from the abstract alone.

### 4. SLaDe: A Portable Small Language Model Decompiler for Optimized Assembly.

*CGO | 2024 | Jordi Armengol-Estapé, Jackson Woodruff, Chris Cummins, Michael F. P. O&apos;Boyle*

> A small seq2seq decompiler paired with type inference targets optimized real-world assembly across ISAs and claims substantially better correctness and readability than both Ghidra and ChatGPT.

Decompiling optimized binaries is still important for security and legacy-porting work, but classic tools often emit hard-to-read code while recent neural methods have mostly stayed on synthetic, unoptimized programs and often fail on correctness. This paper proposes SLaDe, a small sequence-to-sequence Transformer trained on real-world code and augmented with a type inference engine, plus a custom tokenizer and dropout-free regularization. The abstract claims these choices improve both readability and accuracy, including out-of-context type recovery that standard approaches miss. Its evidence is fairly concrete at the abstract level: evaluation on more than 4,000 ExeBench functions across two ISAs and two optimization levels, with reported gains of up to 6x accuracy over Ghidra and up to 4x over ChatGPT. The main missing detail is how accuracy and readability are defined, how much each component contributes, and how well the portability claim extends beyond the tested benchmark settings.

If the claims hold, this suggests a practical model-plus-analysis path for recovering readable and correct code from optimized binaries without depending entirely on brittle hand-engineered decompiler support. A key caveat is that the headline accuracy gains are hard to calibrate because the abstract does not define the evaluation metrics in detail. Another caveat is that portability evidence is limited to two ISAs, so broader cross-architecture generalization remains unproven from the abstract alone.

### 6. FlashAttention: Fast and Memory-Efficient Exact Attention with IO-Awareness

*arXiv preprint | 2022-05-30 | Tri Dao, Daniel Y. Fu, Stefano Ermon, Atri Rudra et al.*

> This paper presents an exact attention algorithm that treats HBM traffic as the main systems bottleneck and reduces it with tiling and kernel fusion.

This paper frames attention as an IO-aware systems problem, with HBM traffic as the key cost to attack. It proposes an exact attention algorithm that uses tiling and kernel fusion to reduce memory movement, which is the mechanism behind its claimed speed and memory-efficiency gains. The abstract also positions the work as the baseline for later FlashAttention variants and many modern attention kernels, so its importance is partly architectural and not just incremental. For LLM systems readers, the main value is the clear design lesson that attention performance depends heavily on data movement, not only arithmetic. The biggest missing detail in the provided abstract is the evidence: it does not state the hardware setup, baselines, workload regimes, or quantitative gains.

If the implementation holds up, the paper provides a reusable kernel-design principle for attention: reduce memory movement first, then build exact high-performance kernels around that constraint. A key caveat is that the provided abstract does not state the hardware assumptions, so portability across GPU generations is unclear. Another caveat is that baseline fairness is not visible from the abstract, so the strength of the empirical claim cannot be checked here alone.
