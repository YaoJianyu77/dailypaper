### 1. Scalable AI Inference: Performance Analysis and Optimization of AI Model Serving

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-22T10:39:14Z | Hung Cuong Pham, Fatih Gedikli*

> A BentoML serving case study that benchmarks a fixed inference workload under realistic traffic, applies stack-level tuning, and reports improved serving behavior without specifying the exact optimization mechanics or gains in the abstract.

Production AI systems often fail on serving efficiency rather than model quality, so latency and throughput behavior under realistic traffic matters. This paper studies a BentoML-based inference stack using a fixed pre-trained RoBERTa sentiment model and evaluates baseline behavior under steady, bursty, and high-intensity workloads generated from gamma and exponential traffic patterns. It then applies optimizations at the runtime, service, and deployment layers and compares the optimized system against the baseline using latency percentiles, throughput, and statistical analysis. The paper also includes a single-node K3s resilience angle, so it reads as a deployment-oriented serving study rather than a model- or kernel-level acceleration paper. The biggest missing detail is that the abstract does not say what the optimizations are, what hardware or configuration is used, or how large the improvements actually are.

If the full paper is methodologically careful, it could be useful as a reproducible serving-stack case study for diagnosing deployment-level latency and throughput tradeoffs, though its direct relevance to LLM decoding or low-level kernel generation is limited. A key caveat is that the study uses a RoBERTa sentiment model, so the conclusions may not transfer cleanly to LLM inference workloads. Another caveat is that the abstract does not disclose the actual optimizations or the size of the gains, which limits assessment of novelty and impact from the summary alone.

### 2. FASER: Fine-Grained Phase Management for Speculative Decoding in Dynamic LLM Serving

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-22T12:44:39Z | Wenyan Chen, Chengzhi Lu, Yanying Lin, Dmitrii Ustiugov*

> This paper turns speculative decoding control from a batch-level, serialized policy into a per-request, overlapped serving mechanism for dynamic LLM workloads.

The paper targets a practical weakness in speculative decoding for online LLM serving: existing systems manage draft and verification too coarsely for volatile traffic, which creates either idle GPU time at low load or wasted verification work at high load. FASER addresses this by choosing speculative length per request inside a continuous batch, pruning rejected tokens early during verification, and splitting verification into smaller frontiers that can overlap with drafting through spatial multiplexing. The abstract claims a vLLM-based prototype improves throughput by up to 53% and reduces latency by up to 1.92x relative to state-of-the-art systems, which is a concrete systems result rather than a purely algorithmic proposal. If the mechanism works as described, the main value is better utilization under changing serving conditions without treating speculative decoding as a rigid batch-wide knob. The biggest missing details are the hardware setup, interference costs of the overlap scheme, and how fair the comparison baselines are across traffic regimes.

For serving systems, the contribution is a more adaptive speculative decoding runtime that may improve both utilization and tail-facing latency behavior when request mix and load fluctuate. A key caveat is that the abstract does not specify the hardware or workload mix, so the reported gains may depend strongly on a particular deployment regime. Another caveat is that spatial overlap between draft and verification may introduce resource contention that is not visible from the abstract alone, even if average gains look good enough overall.

### 3. Distributed Quantum-Enhanced Optimization: A Topographical Preconditioning Approach for High-Dimensional Search

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-22T14:50:21Z | Dominik Soós, Marc Paterno, John Stenger, Nikos Chrisochoides*

> This paper treats near-term quantum hardware as a basin-finding preconditioner for non-convex optimization, then uses a GPU-accelerated classical solver to refine the seeded solutions.

High-dimensional non-convex optimization is hard because the search space grows exponentially, while direct quantum formulations face near-term hardware limits and barren plateaus. The paper proposes D-QEO, which uses a QPU to identify promising basins of attraction and pass high-quality seed points to a GPU-accelerated classical refinement stage. For separable functions, it decomposes a nominal 50-qubit global search into independent 5-qubit subcircuits and runs them concurrently with CUDA-Q, avoiding cross-register entanglement and classical tensor-knitting overhead. The abstract claims gains on 10-dimensional Rastrigin and Ackley benchmarks by avoiding the exponential failure behavior of purely classical methods and reducing downstream BFGS iterations. The main missing detail is whether the end-to-end benefit survives realistic hardware, overhead, and baseline comparisons beyond separable benchmark functions.

If the claim holds, the work suggests a more practical hybrid optimization pattern in which quantum hardware reduces global-search difficulty while classical GPU solvers handle precise local convergence. A key caveat is that the evidence described in the abstract is narrow and limited to Rastrigin and Ackley in 10 dimensions. Another caveat is that the method appears to depend strongly on separable structure, which may sharply limit applicability to harder coupled objectives.
