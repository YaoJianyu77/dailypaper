### 1. Making Every Verified Token Count: Adaptive Verification for MoE Speculative Decoding

*arXiv preprint | 2026-05-01T01:52:01Z | Lehan Pan, Ziyang Tao, Ruoyu Pang, Xiao Wang et al.*

> EVICT adapts MoE speculative-decoding verification by pruning draft-tree prefixes whose expected token benefit does not justify target-side expert activation cost.

Tree-based speculative decoding can lose efficiency on sparse MoE models because wider draft trees make different branches activate different experts, raising target-side verification cost. EVICT is a training-free, hyperparameter-free, lossless method that truncates the draft tree before verification and keeps only the prefix estimated to be cost-effective. The estimate combines fine-grained drafter signals with offline-profiled verification cost, and the abstract says the method is compatible with SGLang. The paper reports experiments across diverse MoE backbones and benchmarks, with up to 2.35x speedup over autoregressive decoding and 1.21x average speedup over EAGLE-3, plus fewer unnecessary expert activations. The biggest missing details are the hardware and serving setup, the exact profiling procedure, and how robust the cost model is across workloads.

If the claims hold, this is a practical serving-side optimization for MoE speculative decoding that targets wasted expert activations without retraining. A key caveat is that the abstract does not disclose hardware, batch sizes, sequence lengths, or request mix, which are central to serving-speed claims. Another caveat is that offline cost profiling may be sensitive to deployment configuration, but the abstract does not establish portability across systems.

### 2. Silicon Showdown: Performance, Efficiency, and Ecosystem Barriers in Consumer-Grade LLM Inference

*arXiv preprint | 2026-05-01T08:45:47Z | Allan Kazakov, Abdurrahman Javat*

> The paper compares consumer Nvidia Blackwell and Apple Silicon paths for 70B+ local LLM inference, arguing that Nvidia's NVFP4 throughput gains collide with VRAM and runtime friction while Apple UMA offers memory headroom and energy-efficiency advantages.

Local LLM inference is moving toward datacenter-scale 70B+ models, which stresses consumer hardware through memory capacity, bandwidth, runtime setup, and power limits. The paper presents an empirical comparison of Nvidia Blackwell and Apple Silicon, highlighting a TensorRT-LLM backend trade-off where NVFP4 improves throughput over BF16 but introduces runtime constraints and startup-latency trade-offs. It also frames a VRAM wall for large models on discrete GPUs, where aggressive quantization or CPU offload can damage quality or throughput, while Apple UMA is claimed to scale more smoothly for 80B models at practical 4-bit precision. The abstract reports concrete throughput, offload, and energy-efficiency numbers, including a 1.6x NVFP4 throughput gain and up to 23x tokens-per-joule advantage for Apple's SoC design. The main missing detail is the full benchmark setup: model set, prompt lengths, decoding settings, power measurement method, quality impact of quantization, and fairness of software baselines.

If the evaluation is methodologically sound, the paper is useful for choosing consumer inference hardware and for understanding when kernel/runtime optimization is less important than memory architecture and ecosystem friction. A key caveat is that the abstract gives headline measurements but not enough benchmark detail to judge fairness across hardware and software stacks. Another caveat is that quantization quality claims may depend strongly on model, task, and evaluation metric, none of which are specified in the abstract.

### 3. VitaLLM: A Versatile and Tiny Accelerator for Mixed-Precision LLM Inference on Edge Devices

*arXiv preprint | 2026-05-01T00:59:46Z | Zi-Wei Lin, Tian-Sheuan Chang*

> VitaLLM is a 16 nm mixed-precision edge accelerator for ternary-weight LLM inference that attacks both projection compute and KV-cache traffic with specialized cores and predictive sparse attention.

Edge LLM inference is constrained by decode latency, memory traffic, and mixed-precision support, especially for ternary-weight models that still require attention and KV movement. VitaLLM combines a multiplier-free TINT core for ternary-INT projections with a BoothFlex core that reuses a radix-4 Booth datapath for INT8 attention and ternary-INT utilization, avoiding duplicated compute arrays. It also uses a leading-one surrogate with a comparison-free top-K selector to prune KV fetches by roughly 1-K/M, while head-level pipelining and an absmax quantization barrier coordinate cross-core execution. The abstract reports a 16 nm, 1 GHz/0.8 V prototype reaching 72.46 tokens/s decode and 0.88 s prefill for 64 tokens within 0.214 mm^2 and 120 KB on-chip memory, with ablations claiming lower KV traffic and better utilization. The main missing detail is how broadly these results hold across model sizes, sequence lengths, accuracy impact from sparse attention, and baseline accelerator comparisons.

If the reported tradeoffs hold, the paper offers a compact hardware blueprint for practical edge-class inference with ternary LLMs and attention-aware memory reduction. A key caveat is that the abstract does not report energy efficiency, accuracy degradation, or detailed baseline comparisons. Another caveat is that sparse attention claims may depend heavily on K, sequence length M, and workload distribution, which are not detailed here.

### 4. SAGA: Workflow-Atomic Scheduling for AI Agent Inference on GPU Clusters

*15 pages, 3 figures, 11 tables. Accepted to HPDC '26 (35th International Symposium on High-Performance Parallel and Distributed Computing), July 13-16, 2026, Cleveland, OH, USA | 2026-05-01T09:05:28Z | Dongxin Guo, Jikun Wu, Siu Ming Yiu*

> SAGA makes an agent workflow, rather than each LLM call, the scheduling unit for GPU-cluster inference so KV-cache reuse and task-level latency can be optimized across chained calls.

This paper targets compound AI workloads where agents issue many dependent LLM calls and request-level GPU schedulers lose reuse opportunities between steps. SAGA shifts scheduling to the workflow level using Agent Execution Graphs for KV-cache reuse prediction, session-affinity batching with work stealing, and a task-completion-time fairness metric. The abstract reports evaluation on a 64-GPU cluster with SWE-bench coding agents and WebArena browser tasks, claiming 1.64x lower task completion time than vLLM v0.15.1 with prefix caching and affinity routing, plus better memory utilization and SLO attainment. The important systems claim is that latency improves by preserving workflow locality and correlated state, not by a faster kernel or model change. The main caveat is explicit: peak throughput is about 30% lower than throughput-optimal batch scheduling, and the abstract leaves implementation overheads and workload generality for the full paper.

If the results hold, this is useful for latency-sensitive agent serving systems where preserving workflow locality is more valuable than peak batch throughput. A key caveat is that the reported 30% lower peak throughput may be unacceptable for throughput-dominated batch services. Another caveat is that results may depend heavily on SWE-bench and WebArena-style agent workflows with reusable intermediate state.

### 5. LLM-Emu: Native Runtime Emulation of LLM Inference via Profile-Driven Sampling

*6 page, 2 figures, workshop paper shape | 2026-05-01T12:35:21Z | Wei Da, Evangelia Kalyvianaki*

> A vLLM-native emulator replaces GPU forward passes with profile-sampled latency while preserving the serving stack needed to approximate online LLM inference behavior cheaply.

Online LLM serving evaluation is expensive because realistic tests need dynamic arrivals, queueing, batching, KV-cache effects, and the serving engine's local scheduler. LLM-Emu keeps vLLM's HTTP, scheduling, KV-cache, and output-processing paths intact, replacing only GPU forward execution with profile-sampled latency and synthetic output tokens. The abstract reports tests across two GPUs, four model variants, two model families, two attention backends, and Poisson plus bursty ShareGPT workloads, with small errors for TPOT, ITL, end-to-end latency, and throughput. TTFT is less stable, which the authors attribute to sensitivity to admission and queue state. The main missing detail for a full read is how profile collection, profile reuse, and workload drift affect accuracy outside the reported setup.

If the reported accuracy holds, this gives systems researchers a cheaper way to test serving policies, load conditions, and runtime behavior before spending GPU time. A key caveat is that tTFT accuracy is explicitly weaker, which matters for admission control and user-visible latency studies. Another caveat is that profile-sampled latency may not extrapolate to unprofiled hardware, model shapes, attention backends, or kernel changes without additional validation.

### 6. Eliminating Hidden Serialization in Multi-Node Megakernel Communication

*arXiv preprint | 2026-05-01T14:18:30Z | Byungsoo Oh, Rachee Singh*

> The paper identifies fence-induced serialization in proxy-based RDMA as the bottleneck that breaks multi-node MoE megakernel scaling and proposes batching and NIC-side ordering to remove it.

Recent MoE megakernels overlap expert compute with fine-grained GPU-initiated communication, but the abstract argues that this single-node advantage collapses across RDMA-connected nodes. The failure mode is hidden serialization in proxy-based transports: each tile transfer must be ordered before its completion signal, forcing fences that drain the NIC pipeline and expose network latency when expert compute is small. Perseus attacks this with decoupled signaling, which batches fences per destination, and NIC-side ordering, which avoids proxy stalls by using hardware fence flags. The abstract reports up to 10.3x end-to-end speedup on proxy-based transports and up to 1.2x over IBGDA GPU-direct, suggesting the bottleneck is ordering serialization rather than proxy transport itself. The missing details are the exact hardware assumptions, workload shapes, and baseline configuration needed to judge how general the result is.

If the evaluation holds, this is directly useful for multi-node LLM inference runtimes that rely on GPU-initiated fine-grained communication and need to keep RDMA latency off the critical path. A key caveat is that the abstract does not specify hardware details, so the NIC-side ordering requirement may limit deployability. Another caveat is that the reported wins may depend on communication-bound MoE shapes with too little expert compute to hide inflated network latency absolute behavior for compute-heavy models is unclear from the abstract alone may be smaller for compute-heavy models than for the highlighted cases simply not addressed by the abstract.
