### 1. PCR: A Prefetch-Enhanced Cache Reuse System for Low-Latency RAG Serving

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-24T10:40:58Z | Wenfeng Wang, Xiaofeng Hou, Peng Tang, Hengyi Zhou et al.*

> This paper presents a RAG serving system that reduces prefill latency by improving KV-cache reuse and hiding cache movement overhead across SSD, DRAM, and GPU.

This paper targets a familiar RAG serving bottleneck: long retrieved contexts make prefill expensive, and naive KV-cache reuse does not help enough once hit rate, transfer cost, and storage latency become the real limits. The proposed system, PCR, combines a prefix-tree cache, a look-ahead LRU policy that uses queued requests, layer-wise overlap between KV loading and GPU compute, and queue-based prefetching from SSD to DRAM. The abstract claims these mechanisms improve reuse efficiency and hide communication latency rather than changing the model itself. Reported evidence is system-level: the authors state that PCR outperforms prior KV-cache reuse methods and reaches up to 2.47x average TTFT speedup. What remains unclear from the abstract is the evaluation setup, hardware assumptions, workload mix, baseline fairness, and how often the gains depend on high prefix overlap in real RAG traffic.

If the method is robust, it could make long-context RAG serving more practical by cutting prefill delay without requiring model changes, especially in workloads with reusable prefixes and storage-backed caches. A key caveat is that the benefit may depend heavily on substantial prefix sharing across requests, which may not hold for many real RAG workloads. Another caveat is that the abstract does not clarify whether the method adds scheduler, memory, or storage-management complexity that could offset operational simplicity gains in practice.

### 2. EchoKV: Efficient KV Cache Compression via Similarity-Based Reconstruction

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-24T07:58:42Z | Yixuan Wang, Shiyu Ji, Yijun Liu, Qingfu Zhu et al.*

> This paper proposes a KV-cache compression method that keeps a reversible path between compressed and standard inference by reconstructing dropped KV residuals from a retained subset.

This paper targets a core long-context inference bottleneck: KV cache memory growth, which limits serving efficiency and model usability at larger context windows. It proposes EchoKV, a similarity-based compression scheme that keeps only part of the KV cache and uses a lightweight network to reconstruct residual components, with the stated goal of allowing on-demand switching between compressed and standard inference instead of locking the model into an irreversible transformed state. The abstract claims that the method exploits inter-layer and intra-layer attention-head similarity and uses a two-stage fine-tuning process that is intended to keep adaptation cost low. Reported evidence is limited to benchmark-level claims on LongBench and RULER plus a brief training-cost example, where the paper says it outperforms prior methods across compression ratios and preserves high throughput in short-context settings. What still needs checking is where the real systems gain comes from in practice, including reconstruction overhead, memory-bandwidth tradeoffs, switching behavior, and whether the baseline comparisons are fair across hardware and serving setups.

If the claims hold, this could offer a more operationally flexible KV-cache compression path for LLM serving, especially in deployments that need to trade memory savings against throughput and revert to standard inference when resources allow. A key caveat is that the abstract does not specify the runtime cost of reconstruction, so net serving gains may be weaker than the compression story suggests. Another caveat is that benchmark claims are underspecified; without metric definitions and baseline details, superiority across compression ratios is hard to judge rigorously.

### 3. Characterizing CPU-Induced Slowdowns in Multi-GPU LLM Inference

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-24T04:06:27Z | Euijun Chung, Yuxiao Jia, Aaron Jezghani, Hyesoon Kim*

> This paper argues that multi-GPU LLM inference can be bottlenecked by CPU-side control work, causing GPU underutilization even when GPU capacity is available.

The paper studies an often-missed systems issue in multi-GPU LLM inference: CPUs may fail to feed the GPUs fast enough, so end-to-end performance degrades despite available GPU resources. It attributes the slowdown to control-side effects such as delayed kernel launches, stalled communication, and higher tokenization latency, and claims these bottlenecks remain even with process separation and CUDA Graphs. The abstract reports that adding CPU cores, which are relatively cheap compared with GPUs, improves stability and reduces time-to-first-token latency by 1.36-5.40x under moderate serving load without adding GPUs. For practitioners, the main value is a configuration warning: GPU provisioning alone is not enough for serving performance. The biggest missing detail is how broad the study is across models, serving stacks, hardware setups, and baseline CPU allocations.

If the results hold broadly, the paper gives a practical serving lesson: modest CPU increases may unlock substantial latency and stability gains without paying for more GPUs. A key caveat is that the abstract does not specify whether the findings depend heavily on a particular hardware topology, runtime stack, or CPU pinning policy. Another caveat is that the reported improvements may partly reflect very CPU-starved baselines, which would affect how general the gains are in well-tuned systems.

### 4. Sparser, Faster, Lighter Transformer Language Models

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-24T13:43:27Z | Edoardo Cetin, Stefano Peluchetti, Emilio Castillo, Akira Naruse et al.*

> This paper argues that extremely sparse feedforward layers can be made practical for transformer training and inference by pairing a new sparse packing format with GPU-oriented CUDA kernels.

This paper targets the main cost center in transformer LLMs: dense feedforward layers that dominate parameters and FLOPs as models scale. It proposes a sparse packing format plus CUDA kernels meant to fit modern GPU execution pipelines so that unstructured sparsity is usable during both inference and training, rather than remaining only a compression result on paper. The abstract also claims a quantitative sparsity study in which simple L1 regularization pushes models past 99% sparsity with negligible downstream degradation, and that these sparsity levels yield better throughput, energy efficiency, and memory use when combined with the proposed kernels. For systems readers, the practical interest is whether the packing and kernel design really convert nominal sparsity into hardware-efficient execution at scale. The biggest missing detail is that the abstract does not make the evaluation setup, baselines, hardware assumptions, or kernel-level source of the gains clear.

If the kernel and format design hold up, this would make extreme sparsity a more credible systems lever for scaling LLM training and especially serving under tighter compute, memory, and energy budgets. A key caveat is that the abstract does not explain how the kernels avoid the usual irregular-memory and load-balance penalties of unstructured sparsity. Another caveat is that the claimed negligible performance impact at over 99% sparsity is strong and needs careful task-level validation in the full paper.

### 5. SparseGPT: Massive Language Models Can Be Accurately Pruned in One-Shot

*International Conference on Machine Learning | 2023-01-02 | Elias Frantar, Dan Alistarh*

> This paper studies one-shot pruning for very large GPT-style models, arguing that high sparsity can be introduced with limited accuracy loss while keeping the result usable for deployment-oriented sparse optimizations.

This paper targets a practical inference problem: reducing the cost of very large GPT-style models through pruning without relying on expensive iterative retraining. The claimed mechanism is one-shot pruning that pushes models to high sparsity while preserving accuracy well enough to remain useful. The abstract further claims that the resulting sparsity patterns stay compatible with downstream deployment optimizations, which is the key systems relevance for inference efficiency. If that holds, the work matters because it connects compression quality to actual serving-side acceleration rather than treating sparsity as a model-only result. The main missing detail is that the abstract does not make clear how accuracy is preserved, what sparsity structure is used, or what deployment gains are demonstrated in practice.

If the method is robust, it could lower the cost of compressing and serving large models by making sparsity practical without a long iterative pruning pipeline. A key caveat is that the abstract does not specify the sparsity pattern, so deployment compatibility may depend heavily on hardware and kernel support. Another caveat is that no latency, throughput, or memory evidence is stated in the abstract, so systems gains are not yet established from the provided text alone.
