### 1. Energy Efficient Software Hardware CoDesign for Machine Learning: From TinyML to Large Language Models

*Accepted as a poster presentation at the EMC2 Workshop, ASPLOS 2026 | 2026-03-24T19:10:42Z | Mohammad Saleh Vahdatpour, Yanqing Zhang*

> A cross-scale review of energy-efficient ML co-design that argues data movement and memory behavior, not raw compute, are the main constraints from TinyML to LLM serving.

This paper is a review of energy-efficient software-hardware co-design across a wide span of ML deployments, from edge TinyML systems to datacenter LLM serving. Its main framing is that energy and performance bottlenecks now come largely from data movement and memory-system behavior rather than arithmetic throughput, so useful optimizations must combine architecture, runtime, and model-level decisions. The survey covers accelerator styles such as ASIC/FPGA dataflows and processing-in-memory or compute-in-memory designs, alongside system techniques including partitioning, quantization, scheduling, and runtime adaptation. It also highlights recurring weaknesses in the area: poor cross-platform generalization, large co-design search spaces, and inconsistent benchmarking across workloads and deployment settings. The practical value is in the synthesis and the proposed hierarchical decomposition perspective, but the abstract does not make clear how systematic the review is or how actionable the framework is in real deployment settings.

If the synthesis is rigorous, it could help systems researchers reason more cleanly about where energy savings come from and which co-design levers transfer from edge inference to LLM serving. A key caveat is that this appears to be a review paper, so there may be limited new empirical systems results. Another caveat is that the abstract is broad and may trade depth for coverage, especially for LLM-serving-specific mechanisms and baselines.

### 2. DVM: Real-Time Kernel Generation for Dynamic AI Models

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-25T12:24:33Z | Jingzhi Fang, Xiong Gao, Renwei Zhang, Zichun Ye et al.*

> This paper proposes a runtime compiler for dynamic AI workloads that replaces conventional machine-code generation with bytecode execution on the target device to cut compilation latency and preserve optimization opportunities.

This paper targets a real systems problem in dynamic AI models: shape and control-flow variation make ahead-of-time compilation brittle, while runtime compilation often costs too much latency and memory. The proposed system, DVM, shifts runtime code generation toward a bytecode virtual machine, where operators are encoded on the CPU and decoded into virtual instructions for direct execution on the NPU, aiming to reduce or hide compilation overhead. On top of that runtime operator compiler, the paper adds static and dynamic graph fusion mechanisms, including pattern-based and stacking-based fusion, to expand optimization opportunities under dynamism. The abstract claims strong gains over TorchInductor, PyTorch eager, and MindSpore graph O0 in both execution efficiency and maximum compilation time, which makes this relevant for inference/runtime co-design. The biggest missing detail is where the reported speedups actually come from in hardware and workload terms, and how comparable the baselines and evaluation setup really are.

If the method holds up, it could make dynamic-model execution substantially more deployable by reducing compile-time stalls while still enabling fusion-based optimization at runtime. A key caveat is that the abstract does not clarify whether the gains depend on a specific NPU execution model or hardware feature set. Another caveat is that the reported speedups may mix compilation-time and execution-time effects in a way that needs closer scrutiny for baseline fairness.

### 3. AVO: Agentic Variation Operators for Autonomous Evolutionary Search

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-25T16:55:04Z | Terry Chen, Zhifan Ye, Bing Xu, Zihao Ye et al.*

> This paper replaces fixed evolutionary search operators with autonomous coding-agent loops and reports attention kernels on NVIDIA B200 that modestly outperform strong hand-engineered baselines.

This paper targets automated discovery of high-performance attention kernels, a setting where most obvious optimization space is already heavily mined by expert implementations. Its core idea is to turn evolutionary variation itself into an autonomous coding-agent loop that can inspect lineage, use domain knowledge, and react to execution feedback to propose, repair, critique, and verify code edits. The abstract reports multi-day autonomous search on multi-head attention over NVIDIA Blackwell B200 GPUs, with kernels that beat cuDNN and FlashAttention-4 on the evaluated configurations, and it also claims relatively fast transfer to grouped-query attention. If the method is real and stable, the value is not just another kernel search pipeline but a stronger mechanism for discovering micro-architectural optimizations without hand-written variation heuristics. The biggest missing detail is evaluation clarity: the abstract does not make baseline fairness, search cost, hardware setup breadth, or reproducibility conditions clear.

If the search cost is justified, this could matter as a compiler-runtime co-design direction for automatically finding kernel-level optimizations that usually require expert manual tuning. A key caveat is that the abstract gives strong performance claims but leaves baseline fairness and reproducibility underexplained. Another caveat is that the reported wins may depend heavily on B200-specific behavior and may not generalize across hardware or workloads clearly from the abstract alone.
