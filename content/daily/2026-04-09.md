### 1. Autopoiesis: A Self-Evolving System Paradigm for LLM Serving Under Runtime Dynamics

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-08T14:37:17Z | Youhe Jiang, Ran Yan, You Peng, Wenshuang Li et al.*

> The paper proposes an online LLM serving system that continuously rewrites its own scheduling and rescheduling policies to adapt to changing runtime conditions.

This paper targets a real systems problem in LLM serving: fixed hand-designed policies degrade when workload mix, cluster size, and runtime trade-offs keep shifting. It proposes Autopoiesis, a self-evolving serving framework in which an LLM synthesizes and rewrites policy code online based on observed system behavior, rather than relying on a static scheduling design chosen before deployment. The abstract claims results across diverse runtime dynamics, with up to 53% and average 34% improvement over state-of-the-art LLM serving systems, which suggests the gains come from adapting policy choices as operating conditions change. For systems readers, the main interest is the shift from offline policy tuning to runtime policy evolution as a control mechanism for serving efficiency. The biggest missing detail is that the abstract does not make clear what policies are editable in practice, how safety and rollback are enforced, or how fair and stable the comparisons are under real serving overheads.

If the method is robust, it could reduce the need for brittle hand-tuned serving heuristics and make LLM serving stacks adapt automatically to changing cluster and workload conditions. A key caveat is that the abstract does not specify whether policy synthesis overhead erodes net serving gains in realistic deployments. Another caveat is that generated policy rewrites may introduce instability, regressions, or unsafe behavior if validation and rollback are weakly designed or too slow to react, so the control loop could harm tail latency or cluster efficiency instead of improving it under adverse conditions alone or together with noisy runtime signals and rapid oscillations that the abstract does not characterize in enough detail to judge robustness confidently.

### 2. StructKV: Preserving the Structural Skeleton for Scalable Long-Context Inference

*Accepted to ACL 2026 Findings, 14 pages | 2026-04-08T07:10:35Z | Zhirui Chen, Peiyang Liu, Ling Shao*

> This paper proposes a structure-aware KV-cache compression method for million-token inference that tries to retain globally important tokens instead of pruning from a single-layer saliency snapshot.

This paper targets a central long-context inference problem: KV cache growth creates both memory-capacity and bandwidth pressure as context windows move toward the million-token regime. The authors argue that common compression methods over-trust local saliency at one layer and can drop tokens that are globally important for information flow across depth. Their method, StructKV, combines depth-aggregated attention centrality, adaptive layer selection for compression, and a decoupling of compute budget from storage budget to preserve the structural skeleton of the context. The abstract claims improved preservation of long-range dependencies and retrieval robustness on LongBench and RULER, which is directionally relevant for LLM serving efficiency. The main missing detail is where the actual efficiency gains come from in system terms, including compression ratio, latency or bandwidth impact, hardware assumptions, and how fair the baseline setup is.

If the method holds up, it could make long-context serving more scalable by reducing KV-cache pressure while avoiding the quality collapse that comes from overly local pruning heuristics. A key caveat is that the abstract does not specify actual throughput, latency, or memory savings, so the systems benefit is still unproven from the provided text. Another caveat is that the added scoring and adaptive-layer logic may introduce overhead that reduces practical gains at inference time, especially at scale.

### 3. MARS: Enabling Autoregressive Models Multi-Token Generation

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-08T12:41:37Z | Ziqi Jin, Lei Wang, Ziwei Luo, Aixin Sun*

> MARS fine-tunes an autoregressive instruction model to emit multiple tokens per forward pass without adding heads or a draft model, aiming to turn predictable token stretches into serving-time throughput gains.

This paper targets a core inefficiency in autoregressive decoding: many next-token runs are predictable, but standard inference still pays one forward pass per token. MARS proposes a lightweight continued-training method that teaches an existing instruction-tuned AR model to produce multiple tokens per step without architectural changes, extra parameters, or a separate speculative draft model. The abstract claims that the same model preserves baseline-level quality in normal one-token decoding, reaches 1.5-1.7x throughput when multi-token acceptance is enabled, and pairs with a block-level KV-cache scheme for up to 1.71x wall-clock speedup on Qwen2.5-7B. The practical systems angle is a runtime confidence threshold that trades speed against quality without model swapping or restart. The biggest missing detail is how token acceptance, threshold calibration, hardware setup, and baseline fairness are implemented and evaluated in full.

If the claims hold, this offers a relatively low-friction path to faster LLM serving with a built-in latency-throughput knob and without the operational cost of draft models or extra prediction heads. A key caveat is that the abstract does not clarify acceptance mechanics, calibration, or failure modes, so quality preservation under distribution shift is still uncertain. ### 4. Measurement of Generative AI Workload Power Profiles for Whole-Facility Data Center Infrastructure Planning

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-08T17:56:41Z | Roberto Vercellino, Jared Willard, Gustavo Campos, Weslley da Silva Pereira et al.*

> This paper builds a public, high-time-resolution power profiling dataset for generative AI workloads and connects it to a bottom-up facility energy model for data center planning.

This paper studies a practical gap in AI infrastructure planning: workload-level GPU power measurements exist unevenly, but operators need facility-level demand estimates to size power and energy systems. The authors measure training, fine-tuning, and inference workloads on NVIDIA H100 GPUs at 0.1-second resolution using MLCommons and vLLM benchmark setups, then feed those profiles into an event-driven bottom-up data center energy model. The claimed contribution is not an inference optimization method, but a measurement and modeling pipeline that turns reproducible workload traces into whole-facility power and energy profiles with realistic temporal variation. If the methodology is sound, it is useful for planning grid connection, on-site generation, and microgrid design around GenAI demand rather than relying on coarse averages. The main missing detail from the abstract is how representative the workloads, user-behavior assumptions, and scaling model are, and how well the facility model is validated against real site measurements.

The practical value is a reproducible way to reason from benchmarked GenAI workloads to site-level power behavior, which could help operators size power delivery and local energy resources more realistically. A key caveat is that the paper is only loosely aligned with inference acceleration or kernel/compiler work; it is a measurement and infrastructure planning study. Another caveat is that the abstract does not show whether the facility model is accurate, calibrated, or robust across different sites and workload mixes.

### 5. InfiniLoRA: Disaggregated Multi-LoRA Serving for Large Language Models

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-08T15:01:04Z | Hongyu Chen, Letian Ruan, Zilin Xu, Yuchen Li et al.*

> This paper proposes a disaggregated multi-LoRA serving design that moves LoRA execution out of the base-model path to improve scalability and tail-latency control, especially when adapter memory grows in newer architectures such as MoE.

The paper targets a concrete serving problem: LoRA is attractive for multi-tenant LLM deployment, but newer architectures such as MoE raise adapter memory cost enough that tightly coupled serving designs become hard to scale and more likely to violate latency SLOs. InfiniLoRA addresses this by disaggregating LoRA execution from base-model inference and introducing a shared LoRA server, parallelism-aware execution, SLO-driven provisioning, GPU-initiated communication, and hardware-specialized LoRA kernels. The abstract claims system-level gains under strict latency targets, including a 3.05x increase in serviceable request rate and a 54.0% increase in the share of adapters meeting the SLO requirement. If those gains hold under realistic workloads, the main value is higher adapter density and better latency isolation for multi-LoRA serving. The biggest missing detail from the abstract is where the improvement comes from quantitatively across communication, kernel execution, provisioning, and hardware assumptions.

For LLM serving systems, the practical payoff would be better multi-tenant scalability and stricter latency control when many adapters must coexist on limited accelerator memory. A key caveat is that the abstract does not separate the impact of architecture-level disaggregation from the impact of low-level kernel and communication optimizations. Another caveat is that the reported gains may depend strongly on specific hardware, traffic patterns, or MoE-related memory pressure that are not described here.

### 6. DeepSpeed Inference: Enabling Efficient Inference of Transformer Models at Unprecedented Scale

*SC22: International Conference for High Performance Computing, Networking, Storage and Analysis | 2022-06-30 | Reza Yazdani Aminabadi, Samyam Rajbhandari, Minjia Zhang, Ammar Ahmad Awan et al.*

> This paper presents a transformer inference system that combines multi-GPU execution, kernel optimization, and heterogeneous CPU/GPU/NVMe memory use to serve very large models under latency and throughput constraints.

This paper targets the systems problem of serving transformer models when model size pushes beyond straightforward single-device deployment. The core idea is a combined inference stack: multi-GPU execution for scale-out, kernel-level optimization for faster execution, and heterogeneous CPU/GPU/NVMe memory support to extend effective model capacity. From the abstract, the claim is that these pieces together enable efficient large-scale inference while respecting latency and throughput constraints, but it does not explain which optimizations matter most or under what hardware assumptions. The practical value is clear for large-model serving, especially where memory capacity is the bottleneck. The biggest missing detail is evaluation clarity: the abstract does not specify baselines, workloads, or how much of the benefit comes from kernels versus parallelism versus offload.

If the integration works as claimed, it offers a practical path to serving larger transformer models by jointly addressing execution efficiency and memory capacity rather than treating them separately. A key caveat is that the abstract is too underspecified to judge baseline quality or the real source of performance gains. Another caveat is that offload-based scaling may depend heavily on specific hardware and interconnect characteristics not described here.
