### 1. DUET: Disaggregated Hybrid Mamba-Transformer LLMs with Prefill and Decode-Specific Packages

*Paper accepted for publication at the Design Automation Conference (DAC) 2026 conference | 2026-03-16T16:56:01Z | Alish Kanani, Sangwan Lee, Han Lyu, Jiahao Lin et al.*

> This paper proposes a phase-disaggregated accelerator for hybrid Mamba-Transformer inference, mapping matrix-heavy prefill and bandwidth-heavy decode to different hardware packages and reporting better TTFT, throughput, and inter-token latency than a B200 GPU.

The paper argues that hybrid Mamba-Transformer LLMs expose a sharper hardware mismatch than standard transformer serving because prefill is compute-heavy while decode is dominated by memory bandwidth, recurrences, and element-wise work. DUET addresses this by splitting inference across two specialized packages: a systolic-array-based prefill package with off-package memory for large matrix multiplications and long-sequence SSMs, and a vector-unit-based decode package with high-bandwidth in-package memory for token-by-token SSM and vector-matrix operations. The design is described as runtime-configurable so mixed Mamba and attention layers can be supported without committing to a single operator profile. The abstract claims evaluation on Nemotron-H-56B, Zamba2-7B, and Llama3-8B across four workloads, with 4x faster time to first token, 1.4x higher throughput, and 1.5x lower time between tokens than a B200 GPU. The main missing details are the hardware cost and power assumptions, the fairness of the B200 comparison, and the runtime overhead of moving work across disaggregated packages.

If the assumptions hold, this gives a concrete accelerator co-design template for reducing both first-token latency and steady-state decode cost in hybrid Mamba-Transformer serving. A key caveat is that the abstract does not disclose area, power, cost, or packaging tradeoffs, so overall efficiency is hard to judge. Another caveat is that baseline fairness is unclear because the abstract does not specify B200 software tuning, batching conditions, or memory configuration details for the comparison setups, so the reported gains may reflect benchmark choices as much as architecture gains. It also leaves open whether the claimed speedups hold under different workload mixes or deployment constraints beyond the four evaluated cases, which affects how broadly the results can be trusted.

### 2. Mamba-3: Improved Sequence Modeling using State Space Principles

*ICLR 2026 | 2026-03-16T17:30:08Z | Aakash Lahoti, Kevin Y. Li, Berlin Chen, Caitlin Wang et al.*

> Mamba-3 combines a more expressive SSM-derived recurrence, complex-valued state updates, and a MIMO design to improve state tracking and downstream accuracy while keeping decode latency flat in the paper's stated formulation.

The paper starts from an inference-first view: Transformers remain strong, but their quadratic compute and linear memory make decode cost a growing bottleneck, while many linear alternatives lose capability or fail to map well to hardware. Mamba-3 addresses this with three changes rooted in the SSM perspective: a more expressive discretization-derived recurrence, a complex-valued state update for stronger state tracking, and a MIMO formulation meant to improve quality without adding decode latency. The abstract claims gains on retrieval, state-tracking, and downstream language modeling tasks. At 1.5B scale, it reports a 0.6-point average downstream accuracy gain over the cited next-best model, another 1.2 points from the MIMO variant, and comparable perplexity to Mamba-2 with half the state size. The main unresolved issue is whether these architectural gains translate into clearly measured hardware efficiency in practice, since the abstract does not specify the serving setup, kernels, or fairness details behind the latency claim.

If the claimed tradeoff holds under real serving conditions, this points to a more practical route to lower-memory, decode-efficient language models that do not give up state tracking or general downstream quality. A key caveat is that the abstract asserts practical inference efficiency but does not specify hardware, kernels, or end-to-end latency measurements. Another caveat is that reported quality gains are hard to interpret without baseline fairness and training-budget details from the full paper.

### 3. LMetric: Simple is Better - Multiplication May Be All You Need for LLM Request Scheduling

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-16T12:43:32Z | Dingyan Zhang, Jinbo Han, Kaixi Zhang, Xingda Wei et al.*

> The paper argues that LLM request scheduling can be reduced to a multiplicative score over a KV-related prefill indicator and current batch size, avoiding the tuning burden of more complex combinators.

Multi-instance LLM serving has to trade off routing a request to an instance with useful existing KV state against keeping load balanced, and prior schedulers often rely on tuned linear combinations or simulator-heavy design. This paper proposes a much simpler rule: multiply two indicators, one derived from how many new prefill tokens the request would need on an instance and one derived from that instance's current batch size. The authors claim this preserves the joint optimization effect while canceling the hyperparameters that usually need tuning, and they report TTFT reductions of 92% and 52% plus TPOT reductions of 21% and 20% against vLLM-v1 and a production scheduler on chatbot, API, and coding-agent workloads. If that holds broadly, the result is a very low-complexity scheduling policy for KV-aware LLM serving. The main missing details are the evaluation setup, baseline tuning fairness, hardware assumptions, and how often the derived failure cases matter in real deployments.

If the claims survive a full read, the main payoff is a simpler LLM scheduling rule that may deliver meaningful latency gains without per-workload parameter search. A key caveat is that the reported gains may depend heavily on workload characteristics, especially how much reusable KV state exists. Another caveat is that the abstract does not specify hardware, model scale, or baseline-tuning details, so fairness is still unverified from the summary alone.

### 4. ExPosST: Explicit Positioning with Adaptive Masking for LLM-Based Simultaneous Machine Translation

*arXiv preprint | 2026-03-16T07:04:55Z | Yuzhe Shang, Pengzhi Gao, Yazheng Yang, Jiayao Ma et al.*

> A SimulMT framework for decoder-only LLMs that avoids positional mismatch by reserving source-token slots and aligning fine-tuning with inference-time decoding.

This paper studies simultaneous machine translation with decoder-only LLMs, where source tokens arrive incrementally and standard positional handling creates a tension between fast KV-cache decoding and positional consistency. It proposes ExPosST, which explicitly reserves fixed positional slots for incoming source tokens so decoding can stay cache-friendly without relying on one specific positional encoding design. The paper also adds a policy-consistent fine-tuning strategy to better match training with inference-time behavior under simultaneous translation policies. The abstract says experiments across multiple language pairs show the method works under diverse policies, but it does not specify the magnitude of the gains, the latency-quality tradeoff, or baseline fairness. The practical value is a cleaner path to streaming translation with decoder-only LLMs, though the systems benefit remains only loosely evidenced at the abstract level.

If the claims hold, the method offers a more generally compatible way to run decoder-only LLMs for streaming translation while retaining efficient cached decoding. A key caveat is that the abstract does not quantify latency, quality, or memory tradeoffs, so the systems benefit is still unclear. Another caveat is that the contribution appears specific to simultaneous translation, and the paper may not generalize to broader LLM serving workloads without further evidence.

### 5. From Threads to Tiles: T2T, a Compiler for CUDA-to-NPU Translation via 2D Vectorization.

*CGO | 2026 | Shuaijiang Li, Jiacheng Zhao, Ying Liu 0055, Shuoming Zhang et al.*

> This paper presents a compiler that rewrites CUDA's thread-level SIMT parallelism into explicit 2-D tile operations for NPUs through structured vectorization.

The paper targets the growing mismatch between CUDA's fine-grained thread model and NPUs that execute coarse-grained 2-D tile instructions. Its main idea is to first lift implicit SIMT parallelism into an analyzable loop representation called Unified Parallelism Abstraction, then choose loop pairs for 2-D vectorization onto NPU tile instructions. The compiler also adds semantics-preserving handling for difficult CUDA features such as control flow and warp-level intrinsics. The abstract reports an implementation on top of Polygeist, results on representative NPU architectures, up to 73% of native CUDA performance on an A100-based comparison, and up to 6.9x over baseline translation methods. The practical value is clear for accelerator portability, but the abstract leaves key details unclear about hardware assumptions, baseline fairness, and which CUDA kernels fail or degrade under this lifting strategy.

If the method generalizes, it offers a compiler path to reuse CUDA kernels on tile-based accelerators without manual rewrites, which is relevant for accelerator portability and low-level code generation workflows. A key caveat is that the abstract does not clarify how broad the supported CUDA subset is, so the effective applicability may be narrower than the framing suggests. Another caveat is that performance is still below native CUDA, and the sources of the remaining gap are not explained in the abstract, which matters for deployment value.

### 6. LLM-VeriOpt: Verification-Guided Reinforcement Learning for LLM-Based Compiler Optimization.

*CGO | 2026 | Xiangxin Fang, Jiaqin Kang, Rodrigo Rocha, Sam Ainsworth 0001 et al.*

> This paper trains a 3B model to generate LLVM IR peephole optimizations with reinforcement learning shaped by Alive2 semantic-equivalence feedback, aiming to make LLM-driven compiler rewrites correct often enough to be usable.

LLM-based compiler optimization is attractive because language models can propose low-level rewrites, but incorrect transformations make the approach unsafe and hard to improve. This paper introduces LLM-VeriOpt, which uses Alive2 semantic-equivalence checks as part of a GRPO reward to train Qwen-3B on LLVM IR peephole optimization. The abstract claims large gains in usable transformations and correctness versus prompt-only Qwen-3B and says the trained model outperforms larger LLM baselines while reaching speedups comparable to LLVM's handwritten instcombine pass, with some emergent cases exceeding it. If those claims hold, the main systems value is a tighter compiler loop where formal verification does not just filter outputs but actively shapes policy learning. The biggest missing detail is evaluation depth: the abstract does not make clear the benchmark mix, verifier failure modes, training data, or how broadly the reported speedups and correctness rates generalize.

If the method is robust, it shows a concrete path for combining formal verification with RL to make small models more viable for low-level compiler transformation tasks. A key caveat is that the abstract does not specify benchmark composition or baseline fairness, so the reported gains are hard to calibrate. Another caveat is that correctness is framed through Alive2 verification, but the abstract does not clarify coverage gaps or unverifiable cases that may matter in practice.
