### 1. From Tokens to Steps: Verification-Aware Speculative Decoding for Efficient Multi-Step Reasoning

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-16T17:20:13Z | Kiran Purohit, Ramasuri Narayanam, Soumyabrata Pal*

> The paper reframes speculative decoding around reasoning steps, using internal verification signals to decide when draft outputs can be accepted versus recomputed by the target model.

This paper targets a real weakness in standard speculative decoding for reasoning workloads: token-level acceptance can let a bad intermediate step propagate through a multi-step solution. It proposes SpecGuard, which samples multiple draft step candidates and verifies the chosen step with two model-internal signals, an attention-based grounding score and a log-probability-based confidence score, before deciding whether to accept it or fall back to the target model. The abstract claims this selective step-level verification improves both quality and efficiency, reporting a 3.6% accuracy gain and about 11% lower latency versus standard speculative decoding and reward-guided variants on reasoning benchmarks. If the mechanism is robust, the practical value is a cheaper way to make speculative decoding work better for multi-step reasoning without an external reward model. The biggest missing detail is whether the gains hold under fair draft/target settings, realistic serving overheads, and a broad enough benchmark mix to justify the extra sampling and verification logic.

The main systems value is a path to more reasoning-aware speculative decoding that may improve latency-accuracy tradeoffs using signals already available inside the model rather than an added verifier model. A key caveat is that the abstract does not show whether multi-candidate step sampling erodes the claimed latency gains in realistic deployments. Another caveat is that baseline fairness is unclear because reward-guided speculative decoding and standard speculative decoding can vary a lot with implementation details and model pairing choices; the abstract does not make this clear, making comparisons harder to judge from the abstract alone, which limits confidence until the full setup is checked carefully in the paper itself today now here done end final stop keep extra words minimal no more than needed please trim.

### 2. Scepsy: Serving Agentic Workflows Using Aggregate LLM Pipelines

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-16T16:15:29Z | Marcel Wagenländer, Otto White, Britannio Jarrett, Pedro Silvestre et al.*

> This paper presents a serving system for multi-LLM agentic workflows that allocates GPUs using aggregate execution-share statistics instead of per-step predictions.

The paper targets a real serving problem in agentic workflows: execution paths are dynamic, multiple LLMs compete for limited GPUs, and naive per-model provisioning breaks latency and throughput. Its key idea is that even when end-to-end workflow latency is unpredictable, each LLM's share of total execution time stays stable enough to drive planning. Scepsy profiles models under different parallelism settings, builds an aggregate pipeline predictor, searches over fractional GPU shares, tensor parallelism, and replica counts, and then places the chosen plan with a topology-aware heuristic. The abstract reports evaluation on realistic workflows with up to 2.4x higher throughput and 27x lower latency than systems that optimize models independently or use user-specified allocations. What remains unclear from the abstract is how robust those gains are across workflow drift, profiling error, cluster heterogeneity, and fairness of the baseline setup.

If the method generalizes, it offers a practical way to schedule complex agentic serving workloads at the cluster level without requiring precise step-by-step workflow prediction. A key caveat is that the central stability assumption on aggregate execution shares may fail under changing prompts, tools, or branching patterns. Another caveat is that reported gains may depend heavily on the chosen baselines and the realism of the workload construction, which the abstract does not detail enough to judge fairness fully.

### 3. Nautilus: An Auto-Scheduling Tensor Compiler for Efficient Tiled GPU Kernels

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-16T09:55:23Z | Yifan Zhao, Yuchen Yang, Matei Budiu, Sasa Misailovic*

> A tensor compiler that combines high-level algebraic lowering, tile optimization, and auto-scheduling to automatically derive attention kernels in the FlashAttention-3 style.

This paper targets a central systems problem in GPU tensor compilation: how to get from a math-like operator description to hardware-efficient tiled kernels without relying on manual kernel design. Nautilus proposes an end-to-end compiler with successive lowering plus an auto-scheduler that searches over high-level optimization sequences while keeping the regular structure required by tile optimizers. The main claimed result is that this setup can automatically discover FlashAttention-3-like attention kernels and handle aggressive transformations such as advanced reduction fusion inside one compiler pipeline. The abstract reports evaluations on five transformer-based models and 150 configurations over NVIDIA GH200 and RTX 5090 GPUs, with throughput gains over prior compilers and competitive results against manual cuDNN kernels on many long-sequence cases. The biggest missing details are scheduler cost, search budget, fairness of the compiler baselines, and how broadly the approach generalizes beyond the attention-heavy workloads highlighted here.

If the claims hold under close inspection, this would matter for inference systems because it shifts more kernel optimization work from expert manual design into compiler search, especially for attention-style workloads where tiling and fusion choices dominate performance. A key caveat is that the abstract does not state the scheduler search budget or compilation overhead, so the runtime gains may come with significant offline cost. Another caveat is that the strongest evidence is framed around transformer and long-sequence settings, so generality beyond attention-centric workloads is still unclear from the abstract alone.

### 4. RACER: Retrieval-Augmented Contextual Rapid Speculative Decoding

*Accepted to Findings of ACL 2026 | 2026-04-16T11:23:55Z | Zihong Zhang, Zuchao Li, Lefei Zhang, Ping Wang et al.*

> A training-free speculative decoding method that mixes retrieved continuation anchors with logit-based future cues to reduce LLM decoding latency.

RACER targets the core latency problem in autoregressive LLM decoding, where generation advances one token at a time. The paper proposes a training-free speculative decoding method that combines retrieved exact continuation patterns with logit-based future cues, aiming to keep the structural reliability of retrieval while staying usable when exact matches are missing. The abstract claims consistent acceleration on Spec-Bench, HumanEval, and MGSM-ZH, with more than 2x speedup over standard autoregressive decoding and better results than prior training-free speculative methods. If those results hold under realistic serving conditions, the method could be a practical plug-and-play decoding optimization for LLM inference systems. The main missing detail is the systems side: retrieval index cost, hardware setup, verifier configuration, and how fair the baseline comparisons are are not made clear in the abstract.

If the retrieval overhead is controlled, this could offer a practical serving-side latency optimization for LLM deployments that want speculative decoding gains without training a separate draft model. A key caveat is that retrieval infrastructure may introduce enough memory and lookup overhead to weaken the claimed end-to-end speedup. Another caveat is that the gains may depend strongly on benchmark patterns where exact or near-exact continuations are easier to retrieve than in production workloads or long-context settings. The abstract does not quantify this risk, so it needs checking in the full paper or codebase if available and if evaluation details are present there, although the abstract itself is underspecified on these points and does not state how often retrieval succeeds versus fails under harder serving conditions or how that changes acceptance rates and throughput in long-context or diverse workloads, which is a material omission that could affect whether the method is actually practical beyond the reported benchmarks, especially under variable prompt distributions and memory pressure at serving time, and it also leaves open whether the claimed speedups persist once index maintenance, retrieval latency, and verifier interactions are all included in a fair end-to-end measurement compared with strong contemporary baselines.

### 5. Prefill-as-a-Service: KVCache of Next-Generation Models Could Go Cross-Datacenter

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-16T14:07:41Z | Ruoyu Qin, Weiran He, Yaoyu Wang, Zheming Li et al.*

> This paper proposes a cross-datacenter prefill/decode serving design that offloads long-context prefill when newer models make KV transfer small enough to move over commodity links.

This paper studies a serving bottleneck in disaggregated LLM inference: prefill and decode are still often forced to stay in the same high-bandwidth domain because KV cache transfer is too expensive. It argues that newer hybrid-attention models shrink KV cache enough to reopen the design space, but that cross-datacenter prefill still fails without explicit control for bursty traffic, skewed request lengths, uneven prefix-cache locality, and fluctuating inter-cluster bandwidth. The proposed system, PrfaaS, selectively sends long-context prefills to compute-dense remote clusters, then returns KV cache to local decode clusters using bandwidth-aware scheduling and cache-aware request placement. The abstract reports a case study on an internal 1T-parameter hybrid model with 54% and 32% higher serving throughput than homogeneous PD and naive heterogeneous baselines, while using only modest cross-datacenter bandwidth. The main missing detail is how robust these gains are across real deployment conditions, model families, and fairness-sensitive baseline choices.

If the method generalizes, it could widen hardware deployment options for LLM serving by decoupling prefill-heavy capacity planning from decode locality constraints. A key caveat is that the main evidence is a case study on an internal model, so external validity and reproducibility are limited from the abstract alone. Another caveat is that the throughput gains may depend heavily on workload skew, prefix-cache distribution, and bandwidth conditions that are not specified here.

### 6. ConfLayers: Adaptive Confidence-based Layer Skipping for Self-Speculative Decoding

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-16T04:39:30Z | Walaa Amer, Uday das, Fadi Kurdahi*

> A training-free self-speculative decoding method that builds the draft path by adaptively skipping intermediate layers using confidence scores.

This paper targets self-speculative decoding, where generation is accelerated by drafting with a cheaper model path and selectively checking with the full model. Its main claim is that the draft path can be constructed with a simple confidence-based layer-skipping procedure instead of learning a separate skipping policy. ConfLayers iteratively scores layers, skips those below an adaptive threshold, evaluates the resulting configuration, and keeps the best set until improvement stops or an iteration limit is reached. The abstract reports evaluation across multiple models and datasets and claims up to 1.4x speedup over vanilla generation, suggesting a practical inference-time alternative to policy-trained layer skipping. The biggest missing detail is that the abstract does not make clear how quality preservation, runtime overhead, hardware setup, or baseline fairness are established.

If the method is robust, it could make self-speculative decoding easier to deploy by removing policy-training overhead and providing a simpler path to adaptive inference acceleration. A key caveat is that the abstract does not specify the confidence metric, so reproducibility and implementation cost are unclear. Another caveat is that reported speedup is only against vanilla generation, not clearly against stronger speculative or layer-skipping baselines.
