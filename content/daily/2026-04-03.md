### 1. Goose: Anisotropic Speculation Trees for Training-Free Speculative Decoding

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-02T13:48:42Z | Tao Jin, Phuong Minh Nguyen, Naoya Inoue*

> This paper argues that training-free speculative decoding should use asymmetric token trees when draft sources have very different acceptance rates, and implements that idea as an adaptive spine-plus-branches structure.

This paper studies a concrete bottleneck in training-free speculative decoding: under a fixed verification budget, tree depth and tree breadth compete, and existing balanced constructions do not account for large quality differences between draft token sources. The authors argue that context-matched n-gram tokens and prior-pass statistical predictions have sharply different acceptance behavior, so the verification tree should be anisotropic rather than balanced. Their method, GOOSE, builds a deep spine from the higher-acceptance context-matched tokens and attaches wider low-confidence branches for alternatives, with a guarantee that accepted tokens per step are at least as large as using either source alone. The abstract reports lossless speedups of 1.9-4.3x across five LLMs and five benchmarks, and says it beats balanced-tree baselines by 12-33% under the same budget. The main detail still needing a full read is how adaptive tree construction, verification cost accounting, and baseline fairness behave across workloads where context matches are sparse or unstable.

If the method is robust, it offers a training-free way to improve speculative decoding throughput by using heterogeneous draft sources more efficiently without changing the base model. A key caveat is that the gains may depend strongly on workloads with frequent high-quality context matches, which may limit generality. Another caveat is that the abstract does not clarify baseline implementation details, so fairness of the budget-matched comparison needs checking in the full paper.

### 2. FlatAttention: Dataflow and Fabric Collectives Co-Optimization for Large Attention-Based Model Inference on Tile-Based Accelerators

*Submitted to IEEE Transactions on Computers. 14 pages | 2026-04-02T14:45:19Z | Chi Zhang, Luca Colagrande, Renzo Andri, Luca Benini*

> This paper presents an attention dataflow for tile-based accelerators that trades HBM traffic for on-chip fabric collectives to raise utilization and improve decoding throughput on large attention-heavy models.

The paper targets a systems bottleneck that is becoming more severe in large-model inference: attention is consuming a growing share of runtime, especially in MoE decoding, while tile-based accelerators expose a strong tradeoff between compute utilization and memory traffic. It proposes FlatAttention, a dataflow that uses collectives in the on-chip network fabric to reduce expensive HBM accesses across modern attention variants rather than treating attention as a kernel-local optimization problem. The abstract reports high tile utilization, much lower HBM traffic, speedups over FlashAttention-3 and GH200-based implementations, and end-to-end throughput and latency gains on DeepSeek-v3 FP8 decoding in a wafer-scale multi-die setup. The practical value is the compiler-runtime-hardware co-design angle: the gains are claimed to come from matching attention data movement to tile interconnect capabilities, not just faster math kernels. The main missing detail is whether the baselines, mapping assumptions, and hardware model make the comparisons fair and reproducible across real accelerator deployments.

If the claims hold, this gives a strong systems path for improving LLM inference on non-GPU tile-based accelerators by turning network-fabric collectives into a first-class optimization surface for attention. A key caveat is that the results may depend heavily on a specific tile-based hardware model and may not transfer cleanly to mainstream GPU deployments. Another caveat is that baseline fairness is hard to judge from the abstract alone, especially for comparisons against FlashAttention-3 and GH200 systems matching different architectural assumptions.

### 3. GPU-RMQ: Accelerating Range Minimum Queries on Modern GPUs

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-02T09:23:08Z | Lara Kreis, Justus Henneberg, Valentin Henkys, Felix Schuhknecht et al.*

> This paper proposes a hierarchical GPU RMQ design that mixes CUDA-core scans with ray-tracing support to reduce memory cost and construction overhead while improving large-array query throughput.

This paper studies range minimum query execution on modern GPUs, where prior GPU and RTX-based methods appear limited by memory overhead, slow index construction, or weak throughput on large inputs. The proposed GPU-RMQ method builds a hierarchy of range-minimum summaries in parallel, then answers queries by scanning only the relevant hierarchy regions with a hybrid use of CUDA cores and ray-tracing cores across levels. The abstract claims stronger throughput than prior GPU and CPU baselines, lower memory footprint, and much faster index construction, with the gains becoming more pronounced on larger arrays. For systems readers, the main interest is the runtime and data-structure co-design: throughput is said to come from restricting query work to relevant hierarchy slices instead of carrying a heavy full index. The biggest missing detail from the abstract is how the hybrid scheduling works in practice, under what workload and hardware assumptions the speedups hold, and how fair the baseline comparisons are.

If the claims hold, the method could make large-scale GPU RMQ more usable in practice by lowering memory pressure and preprocessing cost while preserving high throughput on large inputs. A key caveat is that the topic is adjacent to GPU systems and kernels but not directly tied to LLM inference or code generation, so relevance to the main research focus is indirect. Another caveat is that the abstract gives strong speedup numbers but does not specify workload mix, batching assumptions, or baseline tuning details, so the fairness of the comparison needs checking.

### 4. A Case For Host Code Guided GPU Data Race Detector

*arXiv preprint | 2026-04-02T14:35:49Z | Ajay Nayak, Anubhab Ghosh, Arkaprava Basu*

> This paper argues that GPU race detection improves materially when static analysis uses host-side launch code to constrain the values a kernel can actually receive at runtime.

The paper targets a familiar GPU analysis tradeoff: dynamic race detectors miss bugs that do not manifest in tests, while static tools over-approximate kernel inputs and produce many false alarms. Its core claim is that host code already contains semantic constraints on kernel launch parameters, and that folding those constraints into analysis makes GPU race detection both more complete and more precise. The proposed system, HGRD, performs joint analysis over CPU launch code and GPU kernels rather than analyzing the device code in isolation. The abstract claims stronger results than prior dynamic and static baselines, specifically that HGRD misses no true races considered in the evaluation and avoids the false alarms raised by competing static tools. The main missing detail is how broad that evaluation is, what race patterns are covered, and how robust the approach is to complex host-device interactions in real codebases.

If the method holds up, it suggests a practical compiler-analysis direction for GPU software stacks: use cross-boundary host-device semantics to improve bug finding without overwhelming developers with false alarms. A key caveat is that the abstract gives strong absolute claims, but the benchmark coverage and baseline fairness are not described. Another caveat is that the contribution is about analysis precision, not inference acceleration or kernel generation, so it is adjacent rather than central to the stated LLM systems focus area.

### 5. FlashAttention: Fast and Memory-Efficient Exact Attention with IO-Awareness

*arXiv preprint | 2022-05-30 | Tri Dao, Daniel Y. Fu, Stefano Ermon, Atri Rudra et al.*

> This paper presents an exact attention algorithm that is explicitly optimized for memory IO, using tiling and fused kernel structure to cut HBM traffic.

This paper targets a core systems bottleneck in transformer attention: exact attention is often limited not just by FLOPs but by memory movement between HBM and on-chip resources. It proposes an IO-aware attention algorithm that combines tiling with kernel fusion so intermediate results do not need to be written out and reread in the same way as standard implementations. The abstract supports the main claim that the method reduces HBM traffic while remaining exact, and it positions the work as a foundation for later FlashAttention-style kernels. For LLM systems readers, the practical value is the shift from math-centric optimization to memory-traffic-aware kernel design for attention. The biggest missing detail in the provided abstract is the evaluation setup, including hardware assumptions, sequence regimes, and how much of the gain comes from specific implementation choices versus the algorithmic layout itself.

If the implementation is robust across real workloads, this gives a reusable systems pattern for accelerating attention by co-designing the algorithm with GPU memory hierarchy constraints. A key caveat is that the provided abstract is underspecified on benchmarks, so the magnitude and scope of the gain cannot be judged here. Another caveat is that benefits may depend heavily on GPU architecture, SRAM capacity, and implementation quality rather than the high-level idea alone alone?.
